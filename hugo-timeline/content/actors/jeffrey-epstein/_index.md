## Biography

Jeffrey Epstein was born in Brooklyn in 1953 and, without a college degree, was hired in 1974 to teach calculus and physics at the elite Dalton School in Manhattan under headmaster Donald Barr. After leaving Dalton in 1976, he was brought into Bear Stearns by Alan "Ace" Greenberg, a parent he had met at the school, and rose to limited partner before departing in 1981 amid an SEC insider-trading investigation tied to the Bronfman/Seagram deal. In 1982 he founded J. Epstein & Co., which he described as managing money exclusively for billionaire clients — though no major clients beyond one were ever publicly identified, and the source of his wealth has never been documented (Vanity Fair, March 2003; New York Magazine, October 28, 2002).

Epstein's fortune and access were built largely around a single relationship: Leslie Wexner, founder of The Limited and Victoria's Secret. In July 1991 Wexner granted Epstein sweeping power of attorney over his personal finances and named him a trustee of the Wexner Foundation. The arrangement gave Epstein authority over one of America's largest fortunes and access to Wexner's properties and networks; Epstein acquired Wexner's Manhattan townhouse, later described as the largest private residence in the city. After Epstein's 2019 arrest, Wexner publicly stated that Epstein had "misappropriated vast sums," which he quantified as "at least $46 million" (Wexner Foundation, August 8, 2019; Forbes, August 7, 2019).

By the late 1990s Epstein had assembled a property network — a Palm Beach estate (purchased 1990), the Manhattan townhouse, Zorro Ranch in New Mexico, a Paris apartment, and Little St. James island in the U.S. Virgin Islands (purchased 1998) — and a documented social circle that included Donald Trump, Bill Clinton, Prince Andrew, and Bill Gates. In 2005 Palm Beach police opened an investigation after a parent reported a payment to a 14-year-old; federal investigators ultimately identified at least 36 potential victims. In 2008, under a non-prosecution agreement negotiated by U.S. Attorney Alexander Acosta, Epstein pleaded guilty to two Florida state prostitution charges and served roughly 13 months in county jail with work-release privileges. A federal judge later ruled in 2019 that the agreement violated the Crime Victims' Rights Act by concealing it from victims (DOJ Office of Professional Responsibility report, November 12, 2020).

Epstein was arrested again in July 2019 on federal sex-trafficking and conspiracy charges and died in his Manhattan jail cell the following month, a death officially ruled a suicide. A range of claims that Epstein operated with intelligence protection — including reporter Vicky Ward's account that Acosta said Epstein "belonged to intelligence," and allegations of a Mossad connection by former Israeli intelligence officer Ari Ben-Menashe — remain unconfirmed and disputed; no documentary evidence has been produced in court, and Israeli officials, including former Prime Minister Naftali Bennett, have rejected the Mossad claims (Rolling Stone, "Was Jeffrey Epstein a Spy?," June 22, 2024).
