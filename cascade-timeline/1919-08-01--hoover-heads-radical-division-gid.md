## Opening

Attorney General A. Mitchell Palmer creates the General Intelligence Division (GID) inside the Bureau of Investigation on August 1, 1919, and names 24-year-old J. Edgar Hoover — then a Justice Department clerk with two years' experience in the Alien Enemies Bureau — to head it. Within six months Hoover builds an index of 150,000 individuals and 60,000 organizations considered politically suspect, conducts the [[1919-11-07--palmer-raids-begin-red-scare-deportations]], and establishes the template for systematic political dossier-keeping that will define FBI operations for the next 53 years.

## What Happened / Key Facts

The GID was stood up in response to a May-June 1919 wave of anarchist mail bombs, including a June 2 bomb that destroyed part of Palmer's Washington home. Palmer's response was dual: create a domestic intelligence apparatus and conduct mass deportation sweeps.

Key facts:

- **Hoover's card-index system** initially covered 150,000 names. By 1921 it reached 450,000. By 1940 approximately 10 million. By Hoover's death in 1972 the files covered tens of millions.
- **Subject coverage was political, not criminal.** Files included union members, civil-liberties lawyers, civil-rights activists, suffragists, journalists, clergy, academics, Members of Congress, Supreme Court Justices, and — after 1933 — FDR's cabinet and eventually FDR himself.
- **No statutory authorization.** Congress never passed a law authorizing general-purpose political dossier-keeping by the FBI. The GID operated on the same DOJ administrative-authority doctrine that created the Bureau in 1908.
- **Hoover's methods drew from Pinkerton.** Indexed subject files, informant networks, undercover infiltration — all established private-detective techniques absorbed into federal law enforcement.

## Why This Event Matters

The GID is the institutional germ of every subsequent U.S. domestic surveillance scandal:

- **Personnel-file-as-leverage template.** Hoover accumulates material on political figures and uses it to secure his job, budgets, and policy preferences across eight presidencies. The template is replicated by Roy Cohn ([[1953-02-09--roy-cohn-systematic-blackmail-infrastructure]]) and directly inherited by later operations.
- **Classification as protection.** The files are maintained in "official and confidential" and "personal and confidential" categories never subject to congressional or judicial oversight until the [[1975-01-27--church-committee-begins]] investigations. This is the precursor to every compartmentalized intelligence program from MKUltra forward.
- **Target-list logic.** The GID index is the ancestor of the FBI Security Index (1939-1971), the NSA MINARET watchlists (1967-1973), the Terrorist Screening Database (2003-present), and the post-2025 DHS/ICE deportation target lists. Each generation's target list operates on the same basic mechanic: an administrative agency generates a name list with no judicial review and uses it to direct enforcement.

## Broader Context

When Hoover is appointed Acting Director of the Bureau of Investigation in 1924 ([[1924-05-10--hoover-appointed-fbi-director]]), he brings the GID's files and methods to the Bureau's central operations. From that moment the political-dossier function becomes the core FBI activity, not a subsidiary one — a structural reality that the 1975 Church Committee will document but not fully dismantle.
