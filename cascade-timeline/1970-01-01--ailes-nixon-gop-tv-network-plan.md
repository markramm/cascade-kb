In the summer of 1970, a 15-page Republican memo titled **"A Plan For Putting the GOP on TV News"** circulated inside the Nixon White House, addressed to chief of staff **H.R. Haldeman**, with media consultant **Roger Ailes'** handwriting in the margins (his notes responding back to Haldeman). The plan proposed building an operation to produce **prepackaged, pro-administration video news stories** and feed them directly to local TV stations — bypassing the three national networks, which Ailes and Nixon regarded as irredeemably liberal — at an estimated startup cost of ~$310,000. Its premise, stated plainly, is the document's most-quoted line: *"Today television news is watched more often than people read newspapers... The reason: People are lazy. With television you just sit—watch—listen. The thinking is done for you."* The memo surfaced publicly only in 2011, when journalist John Cook reported it at Gawker from documents held in the Nixon and Bush presidential libraries.

The blueprint was partly executed a few years later as **Television News Incorporated (TVN)**, the conservative video-news service financed by beer magnate **Joseph Coors** (~1973–1975), which injected pro-administration segments into local newscasts before folding. The full vision — a nakedly partisan national news channel built to circumvent the accountability press — arrived 26 years after the memo as **Fox News** (1996), launched by Rupert Murdoch with Ailes at its head. The 1970 memo is therefore the documentary origin point of the **media-capture lane**: the explicit strategy to manufacture and distribute pre-thought "realities" to voters in order to neutralize independent journalism. Its logic — that television does the audience's thinking, and that this can be weaponized to blunt accountability — is the through-line from the Nixon enemies-list era to the modern partisan-media ecosystem, and the precondition for the claim, made by JD Vance at the Nixon Library in June 2026, that Watergate "would be a 12-hour news story" today: the foreshortened news cycle Vance celebrates is the one this memo set out to build.
