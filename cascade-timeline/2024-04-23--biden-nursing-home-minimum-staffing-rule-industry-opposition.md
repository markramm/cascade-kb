Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services finalizes first-ever national minimum staffing requirements for nursing homes receiving Medicare and Medicaid funding: every resident must receive at least 3.48 hours of nursing care daily, including minimum 0.55 hours from registered nurses and 2.45 hours from nurse aides, with RN on-site 24/7. The rule faces immediate fierce opposition from the nursing home industry and Republican officials who argue it will force facility closures despite overwhelming evidence that understaffing kills residents. Industry trade groups American Health Care Association and LeadingAge sue to overturn the regulation, while 20 Republican state attorneys general file separate legal challenge. In November 2023, Iowa Governor Kim Reynolds and 14 other Republican governors send Biden a letter claiming the rule will 'force over 80% of facilities nationwide to hire more staff' and cause closures, particularly in rural areas. Industry calls it an 'unconscionable' 'one-size-fits-all requirement' that will 'exacerbate workforce crisis' and 'jeopardize access to care for seniors,' rhetoric that ignores how systematic understaffing by profit-maximizing owners already jeopardizes patient lives. CMS offers waivers for 24/7 RN requirement and 'financial hardship exemptions' for facilities in areas where nurse supply is 20%+ below national average, with estimated 25% of facilities qualifying for rural area exemption, though industry claims waivers are insufficient. Requirements to be phased in over 2-5 years. The rule addresses crisis exposed by COVID-19 and private equity research showing understaffing directly causes deaths, yet industry prioritizes profit extraction over patient safety. In June 2025, federal judge in Iowa vacates Biden's staffing mandate, and Trump administration HHS moves to roll back requirements. The regulatory battle reveals how nursing home industry captures regulators and politicians to block patient protection measures, prioritizing owner profits over preventing deaths of vulnerable elderly Americans. Industry's success in blocking lifesaving staffing standards demonstrates regulatory capture that enables continued profit extraction through understaffing.
