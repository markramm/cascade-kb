On January 20, 2026, five Minnesota physicians held a hastily convened press conference at the State Capitol in St. Paul to document how the federal immigration surge known as Operation Metro Surge was driving patients out of clinics and hospitals and endangering their lives. They were flanked by two Democratic-Farmer-Labor state senators who are themselves physicians, Sen. Alice Mann and Sen. Matt Klein. Among the speakers were Dr. Erin Stevens, legislative chair for the Minnesota section of the American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists (ACOG), and Dr. Janna Gewirtz O'Brien, president-elect of the Minnesota chapter of the American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP). Mann compared the moment to the pandemic: "The only other time in history I can remember having a group of physicians standing in front of you (like this) was during COVID... this is a moment of crisis in our state."

The physicians described concrete harms traceable to fear of ICE rather than to disease. Dr. Stevens reported that requests for home births were rising, even among women with higher-risk pregnancies, while prenatal-visit attendance was falling; in one case a nurse found a third-trimester patient who had stopped coming to appointments laboring alone, eight centimeters dilated and too frightened to seek help. Other cases included a diabetic patient rationing insulin and running low on food to avoid leaving home, a burst appendix that festered untreated for days, and a jaundiced infant who missed appointments. The doctors also invoked the tear-gassing of a six-month-old who was hospitalized with breathing problems during the north Minneapolis operation. Sen. Klein connected the surge to the January 7 killing of Renée Good by ICE agent Jonathan Ross, noting that a physician who arrived at the scene was rebuffed when offering aid and that Good still had a pulse roughly eight minutes after the shooting.

Structurally, the press conference is an instance of a captured-enforcement apparatus generating its own collateral public-health crisis, then having that crisis named by the very professionals the regime's "sensitive locations" rollback was supposed to protect. The deployment of sitting physician-legislators as messengers, and the framing of medical authority against federal force, marks the point at which a normally apolitical professional class is pushed into open political opposition. The Renée Good detail — a credentialed doctor turned away from a dying woman by armed agents — collapses the distinction between immigration enforcement and the denial of emergency care, which is the chilling effect operating not as deterrence but as direct lethal consequence.
