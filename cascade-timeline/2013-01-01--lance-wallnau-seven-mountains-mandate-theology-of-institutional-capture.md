Lance Wallnau, a leadership consultant and self-described "strategic advisor," popularizes the "Seven Mountains Mandate" to a mass evangelical audience — the theology that Christians must gain dominion over seven spheres of cultural influence: (1) Religion, (2) Family, (3) Education, (4) Government, (5) Media, (6) Arts & Entertainment, and (7) Business. The framework originates from a 1975 meeting between Loren Cunningham (founder of Youth With A Mission) and Bill Bright (founder of Campus Crusade for Christ), who independently claim God revealed the same seven spheres to each of them simultaneously.

C. Peter Wagner — a former Fuller Theological Seminary professor who coined the term "New Apostolic Reformation" (NAR) around 2000 to describe a movement of self-appointed apostles and prophets who claim direct revelatory authority — adopts the Seven Mountains framework as NAR's organizing principle. But it is Wallnau who translates it from charismatic insider language into a political action framework that mainstream evangelicals can adopt. Through books, conferences, and social media, Wallnau reframes the Seven Mountains not as abstract theology but as a strategic plan: identify the institutions that shape culture, infiltrate them, and establish Christian leadership at every level.

The Seven Mountains Mandate is, in secular terms, an explicit theology of institutional capture. It names the institutions to be captured (government, media, education, business, arts, family, religion), defines success as achieving leadership positions within those institutions, and provides theological justification for prioritizing institutional power over individual conversion. Rushdoony's Christian Reconstruction (1973) provided the intellectual framework; Schaeffer (1976) provided the moral urgency; the Moral Majority (1979) and CNP (1981) provided the organizational infrastructure. The Seven Mountains Mandate provides the strategic map.

Wallnau becomes one of the earliest evangelical leaders to endorse Trump (2015), declaring him a "wrecking ball to the spirit of political correctness" and later calling him a "modern Cyrus" — the Persian king who, without being a believer himself, served God's purposes by liberating the Jews from Babylonian captivity. This "Cyrus theology" gives evangelicals permission to support a manifestly irreligious candidate by reframing the relationship: Trump is not a Christian leader but a divinely appointed instrument for Christian institutional capture.
