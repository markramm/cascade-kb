On the morning of April 7, 2026 — day 38 of the war with Iran — President Trump posts on Truth Social: "A whole civilization will die tonight, never to be brought back again. I don't want that to happen, but it probably will." Trump has issued an ultimatum: Iran must agree to reopen the Strait of Hormuz by 8 PM Eastern, or the United States will destroy all of Iran's power plants and bridges.

The post continues: "However, now that we have Complete and Total Regime Change, where different, smarter, and less radicalized minds prevail, maybe something revolutionarily wonderful can happen, WHO KNOWS? We will find out tonight, one of the most important moments in the long and complex history of the World. 47 years of extortion, corruption, and death, will finally end. God Bless the Great People of Iran!"

The threat comes hours after U.S. forces struck dozens of military targets on Kharg Island — Iran's primary oil export terminal — overnight, though the strikes reportedly avoided energy infrastructure. Iran rejected a U.S. ceasefire plan the previous day and countered with a proposal for a permanent end to the war. Trump called Iran's counter-proposal "not good enough" but "a very significant step."

Iranian state media reports that civilians are forming human chains around power plants in response to Trump's threat. The image — of a civilian population attempting to physically shield their infrastructure from American bombing — is the direct consequence of threatening civilian infrastructure as a matter of declared policy.

The deliberate destruction of civilian power plants and bridges would constitute a war crime under multiple frameworks: Article 54 of Additional Protocol I to the Geneva Conventions prohibits attacking "objects indispensable to the survival of the civilian population"; the Pentagon's own Law of War Manual restricts attacks on civilian infrastructure; and the Rome Statute of the International Criminal Court classifies intentional attacks on civilian objects as war crimes.

The threat lands on the same day The RAMM publishes "Hegseth Removed The Circuit Breakers," documenting that every senior military and intelligence official whose institutional role included the authority to push back on unlawful orders has been removed since January 2025. The thirteen fired leaders include the Chairman of the Joint Chiefs, the Army Chief of Staff (fired five days earlier for refusing to strip promotions from Black officers), the NSA director, the DIA director, and the top military lawyers for the Army, Navy, and Air Force — the Judge Advocate General officers whose specific function is advising commanders whether orders constitute war crimes.

There are no JAGs at the top of the chain whose institutional role requires them to say: destroying civilian power infrastructure serving 88 million people is a war crime. There is no DIA director whose name goes on the damage assessment. There is no Joint Chiefs chairman who can advise the president that the order exceeds military necessity. The circuit breakers have been removed. The current is surging.

Five former defense secretaries have called on Congress to exercise oversight. Senator Tammy Duckworth has warned that "installing loyalists at the top of the military is the kind of thing autocrats do." But as of 8 AM on April 7, the question is not whether institutional guardrails will function. The question is whether anything remains in the system capable of saying no before 8 PM.
