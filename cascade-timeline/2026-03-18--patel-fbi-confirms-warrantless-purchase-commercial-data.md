On **March 18, 2026**, testifying before the Senate Select Committee on Intelligence, FBI Director **Kash Patel** confirmed that the Bureau **"purchases commercially available information,"** and — asked directly whether he would commit to not buying Americans' location data without a warrant — declined to do so.

The statement is significant on three counts:

1. **It reverses prior FBI testimony.** Patel's predecessor told Congress in 2023 in terms that left the Bureau's reliance on purchased commercial data ambiguous or denied; Patel's confirmation makes the practice official and on the record.

2. **It confirms the "data-broker loophole" at the highest level.** *Carpenter v. United States* (2018) held that the government needs a warrant to **compel** long stretches of phone location data from a third party. It said nothing clear about what the government may **buy**. Agencies — CBP (airline flight records via a broker, under a contract barring naming the source), ICE (the Thomson Reuters CLEAR utility database; 1M+ LexisNexis searches in a seven-month 2021 stretch) — have exploited that gap by purchasing what they would otherwise need a warrant to obtain. Patel's admission confirms the FBI is part of that pattern.

3. **It is the live news peg for the legislative fix.** The **Fourth Amendment Is Not For Sale Act**, which would ban the government from buying the data it would otherwise need a warrant to get, passed the House 219–199 in April 2024 but died in the Senate and (as of mid-2026) has not been reintroduced. Patel's confirmation is the kind of on-record government admission that re-opens the case for it.

Part of the same March-2026 news cluster as the CDT/Anthropic disclosure (the Pentagon's interest in commercial bulk data; see related event).
