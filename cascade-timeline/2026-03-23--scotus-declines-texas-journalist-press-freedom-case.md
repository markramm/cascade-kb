On March 23, 2026, the Supreme Court declined to hear the case of Priscilla Villarreal, known online as "La Gordiloca," a citizen journalist in Laredo, Texas, who was arrested in 2017 for publishing stories that included information sourced from a local police officer. The court's refusal to grant certiorari left in place a Fifth Circuit en banc ruling that had reversed an earlier panel decision favorable to Villarreal, effectively allowing Texas's unusually broad information-solicitation statute to be used against journalists.

The Texas law under which Villarreal was prosecuted makes it a felony to solicit non-public information from a government official with the intent to obtain a benefit — a statute so broadly written that press freedom organizations argued it could criminalize routine newsgathering. Villarreal, who ran a popular Facebook page covering crime and local government in Laredo, had obtained information about a suicide and a Border Patrol agent's death from a police officer who provided it voluntarily. She was arrested, booked, and charged with two felonies.

Justice Sonia Sotomayor authored a pointed dissent from the denial of certiorari. "It should be obvious that this arrest violated the First Amendment," she wrote, arguing that the case presented a question of enormous significance — whether the government can criminalize the act of asking a willing government source for information — that the Court had a responsibility to address. Sotomayor warned that the Fifth Circuit's ruling created a framework under which any journalist who cultivated a government source could potentially face felony prosecution.

The Court's refusal to intervene landed at a moment of acute concern over press freedom in the United States. Coming just days after a federal judge struck down the Pentagon's press access policy and amid ongoing controversy over the detention of journalist Estefany Rodriguez, the Villarreal decision reinforced fears that the legal protections for newsgathering were eroding from multiple directions simultaneously. First Amendment scholars noted that while the case originated before the current administration, the Court's decision to leave the Fifth Circuit ruling intact provided a legal template that could be used to target journalists in a political environment increasingly hostile to adversarial reporting.
