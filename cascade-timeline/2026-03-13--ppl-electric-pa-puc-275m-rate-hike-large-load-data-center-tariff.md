A PPL Electric settlement filed **March 13, 2026** at the Pennsylvania Public Utility Commission — effective July 1, 2026 — locks in a **$275 million** annual rate increase (a 4.9% residential bill rise to roughly $184/month) alongside a new **Large Load Tariff** requiring data centers drawing 50+ MW peak demand to bear grid-expansion costs. It is a precedent-setting test of whether hyperscalers pay their own infrastructure costs: PPL's interconnection pipeline now holds roughly **20 gigawatts** of contracted large loads against a 7.8 GW peak system — a near-tripling of demand in 5-6 years. Ratepayer advocates secured $11M/year in low-income protections and stranded-cost recovery if operators abandon facilities, but the baseline increase means eastern Pennsylvania residential customers already bear costs of a buildout they had no voice in.

This is the utility-regulation front of the data-center cost-shift the timeline tracks through the record PJM capacity auction ([[2025-11-01--pjm-capacity-auction-record-data-center-40-percent-costs]]), Shapiro's PA incentive package ([[2026-06-02--shapiro-releases-pennsylvania-data-center-incentive-conditions-2b-exemption]]), and parallel PUC proceedings in Oregon ([[2026-06-10--oregon-puc-delays-pge-data-center-large-load-rate-ruling]]) and Texas ([[2026-06-10--abbott-directive-puct-ercot-data-center-infrastructure-costs]]). The open question is whether the Large Load Tariff creates true cost-separation or merely defers costs — and whether other state PUCs cite it as the national model for large-load cost allocation.
