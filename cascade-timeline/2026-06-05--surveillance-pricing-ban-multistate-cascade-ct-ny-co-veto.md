# Surveillance-Pricing Bans Move Across the States

In late May and early June 2026, the fight over **surveillance pricing** — the practice of using personal data (browsing, location, device, purchase history) to set a *customized* price for each individual buyer — broke into state law on three tracks at once:

- **Connecticut** enacted a ban: **SB 4**, signed **May 27, 2026**, effective **October 1, 2026**, as part of a broader privacy overhaul. It prohibits retailers and delivery services from setting customized prices based on personal data. (EPIC: "Connecticut Is Second State to Enact Surveillance Pricing Ban.")
- **New York** lawmakers **approved** a surveillance-pricing ban (~**June 5, 2026**) amid heavy industry opposition — Uber and others lobbying that it would "kill promotions" and raise costs. (EPIC: "New York Becomes Third State to Pass Surveillance Pricing Ban.")
- **Colorado** Governor **Jared Polis vetoed** his legislature's ban (veto letter **June 2, 2026**), citing a too-broad approach that could "discourage valid uses of technology." The bill had passed both chambers; Consumer Reports had publicly called on Polis to sign.
- **California**'s ban bill was advancing in the same window (EFF: "Yes to California's Bill to Ban Surveillance Pricing").

By mid-2026, state trackers (MultiState.ai, State of Surveillance) counted roughly **24 states** moving on surveillance-pricing legislation, with **Maryland, Colorado, and Connecticut** leading the 2026 wave.

## Why it is structurally significant

Surveillance pricing is the consumer-facing edge of the **surveillance-advertising / data-broker** apparatus the corpus documents on the demand side: the same personal-data exhaust that powers RTB and data-broker resale is here turned to **price discrimination at the individual level** — charging *you* more because the data says you'll pay it. It is data colonialism applied directly to the wallet.

The state-by-state pattern is the load-bearing part. This is a **multi-state legislative cascade** — the precise "structural escalation" shape the pipeline is built to catch: not one bill, but a wave (CT enacted / NY approved / CO vetoed / CA pending / ~24 tracking), with the **industry counter-mobilization visible in the splits** (Uber lobbying NY; Polis's veto echoing the industry's "chilling valid uses" framing). The vetoes and lobbying map the resistance the surveillance-advertising industry mounts when its pricing layer is threatened — which is itself evidence of how load-bearing that layer is to the model.

This is convergence, not coordination, on the *reform* side: 24 statehouses independently reaching for the same remedy because the same extractive practice reached them all. On the *industry* side, the opposition is more coordinated (the same firms, the same "kills discounts" script across states).
