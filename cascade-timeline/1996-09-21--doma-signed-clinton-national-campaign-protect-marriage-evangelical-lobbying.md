President Clinton signs the Defense of Marriage Act on September 21, 1996, defining marriage in federal law as solely between a man and a woman and allowing states to refuse recognition of same-sex marriages performed in other states. Clinton signs it overnight, avoiding ceremony — he supported the Hawaii Supreme Court case that had raised the possibility of state-recognized same-sex marriage, and he feared the electoral cost of either position in a reelection year.

**The lobbying campaign that produced DOMA:** By late 1995 and early 1996, a broad coalition of national conservative Christian groups — the American Family Association, Christian Coalition, Concerned Women for America, Eagle Forum, Family Research Council, Focus on the Family, and Traditional Values Coalition — launches a coordinated nationwide legislative campaign to federalize the marriage definition before any state could legalize same-sex marriage. In January 1996, the coalition formalizes as the National Campaign to Protect Marriage (NCPM), organized at meetings of Christian right groups in Memphis, Tennessee. The campaign is a textbook example of the Christian right's new 1990s sophistication: multi-organization coalition, Washington lobbying arm (FRC), media operation (Dobson's radio network), grassroots mobilization (Christian Coalition voter-guide infrastructure), and framing discipline ("defense of marriage," not "anti-gay").

**The structural significance:** DOMA is the Christian right's first major federal legislative win on a culture-war issue since the Reagan years — and it comes under a Democratic president who signed it to neutralize the issue in an election year. The coalition achieves legislative capture not by electing sympathetic legislators (the 1994 Republican Congress made that easier) but by making the issue so salient that the opposing party's president signs their bill to avoid electoral punishment.

DOMA also demonstrates the marriage-defense strategy that will define the 2000s: use federal and state legislation to lock in a definition before courts can extend equal protection. The DOMA playbook — "don't let the courts decide, pass a law first" — is the same logic that produces the Federal Marriage Amendment push (2003-2004), the 11-state anti-gay-marriage ballot initiative strategy timed to boost evangelical turnout in the 2004 Bush reelection, and the Republican Party's consistent use of LGBTQ issues as mobilization-first, policy-second instruments through the 2020s.

DOMA passes the House 342-67 and the Senate 85-14 — bipartisan supermajorities that reveal how thoroughly the Christian right's marriage-defense framing has captured the political center. Clinton's signing — "divisive and unnecessary" in his own words — marks the moment when the culture-war coalition demonstrates it can win legislative victories even without presidential allies.
