In his February 24, 2026 State of the Union address — the longest in recorded history at nearly two hours — President Trump delivered a series of statements framing the United States as a divinely chosen nation undergoing a Christian religious revival, using language that religious studies experts characterized as distinctly different from the generic civil religion of previous presidents.

Trump declared that since taking office the United States had experienced "a tremendous renewal in religion, faith, Christianity and belief in God." He attributed American national achievements to divine selection, stating "when God needs a nation to work his miracles, he knows exactly who to ask." In tribute to recently deceased conservative activist Charlie Kirk — whom Trump awarded the Presidential Medal of Freedom — he called on Americans to "reaffirm that America is one nation under God." He framed American destiny as "written by the hand of Providence."

Religious studies scholars and civil liberties advocates noted the theological specificity of the address. Melissa Deckman, CEO of the Public Religion Research Institute, observed the speech contained both overt religious references and "smaller, more subtle" nods to Christian nationalist sentiments. Barbara Perry of the University of Virginia's Miller Center stated the address "was meant to appeal to a base largely composed of white evangelical Christians," contrasting it with the more ecumenical spiritual language of earlier presidents. Ryan Burge, a professor at Washington University's John C. Danforth Center on Religion and Politics, directly disputed Trump's revival claim: "There's just no empirical evidence that points to that conclusion."

Rev. Paul Raushenbush, president of Interfaith Alliance, characterized the administration as "the most hostile to religious freedom in generations" and accused it of having "weaponized religion for their white Christian nationalist crusade." Interfaith Alliance had filed suit just two weeks earlier challenging the constitutionality of the Trump-created Religious Liberty Commission.

The SOTU's religious framing was not incidental rhetorical flourish. It was delivered simultaneously with the administration's active deployment of multiple institutional mechanisms to embed Christian nationalist priorities in federal governance: the White House Faith Office, the Task Force to Eradicate Anti-Christian Bias chaired by Attorney General Bondi, new Department of Education guidance on school prayer issued February 5, and Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth's ongoing program of monthly Christian worship services at the Pentagon. The State of the Union provided the ideological capstone narrative — a presidential declaration that Christian revival was a measurable, government-affirmed national achievement — to accompany the bureaucratic infrastructure already being built.
