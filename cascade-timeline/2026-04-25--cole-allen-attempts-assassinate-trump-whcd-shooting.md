At approximately 8:40 p.m. on April 25, 2026, Cole Tomas Allen, a 31-year-old Caltech-educated mechanical engineer from Torrance, California, approached a security checkpoint on the Terrace Level of the Washington Hilton — the venue hosting the White House Correspondents' Dinner — and ran through the magnetometer holding a 12-gauge pump-action shotgun. A U.S. Secret Service officer, wearing a ballistic vest, was shot once in the chest. The officer drew his service weapon and fired multiple times at Allen, who fell to the ground with minor non-gunshot injuries. President Trump, First Lady Melania Trump, Vice President JD Vance, and Cabinet members were evacuated by Secret Service. Allen had purchased the shotgun on August 17, 2025; he also possessed a .38 caliber semi-automatic pistol purchased October 6, 2023. He had driven cross-country from California with the weapons.

Per Ken Klippenstein's reporting (April 26, citing a senior FBI official), Allen had not been flagged by the FBI's domestic counterterrorism apparatus. Acting Attorney General Todd Blanche confirmed Sunday: "There's not any indication yet" that Allen was on law enforcement's radar. Allen left a 1,000+ word manifesto invoking Christian theology to justify the attack — addressing turning-the-other-cheek and "render unto Caesar" passages — and identifying his targets as administration officials "prioritized from highest-ranking to lowest, *not including Mr. Patel*." Allen was a graduate of the Caltech Christian Fellowship and served as president of Caltech's Nerf club, where he reportedly resisted realistic weapon modifications. He had compared Trump to the Antichrist on Bluesky on April 13.

The Washington Hilton was the site of the 1981 Reagan assassination attempt. Video evidence reviewed by reporters showed Secret Service and DC Metropolitan Police were inattentive as Allen ran past the magnetometer with a long gun. The case is being investigated by the FBI Washington Field Office. Allen was arraigned April 27 on three counts: attempt to assassinate the President, transportation of a firearm and ammunition in interstate commerce with intent to commit a felony, and discharge of a firearm during a crime of violence. The attack occurred during the FBI's most public crisis of confidence in modern memory — eight days after The Atlantic's reporting on Patel's drinking and absences (April 17), four days after the SPLC indictment (April 21), and one day after the Intercept surfaced Patel's 2005 alcohol-arrest disclosure (April 24).
