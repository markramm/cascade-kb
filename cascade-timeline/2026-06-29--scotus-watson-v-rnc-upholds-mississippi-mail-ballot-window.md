On **June 29, 2026**, the Supreme Court ruled **5-4** in *Watson v. Republican National Committee* that Mississippi's 2020 law permitting mail-in ballots **postmarked by Election Day and received within five days** may stand. Justice **Amy Coney Barrett** authored the majority opinion, joining Chief Justice Roberts and Justices Sotomayor, Kagan, and Jackson; the majority reasoned that the federal Election Day statutes fix when voting must occur but do not set a ballot-receipt deadline. The decision blocks the RNC's effort to invalidate late-arriving mail ballots and is a rare Trump-side loss at the Court on election administration.

This is a counter-current in the election-administration lane otherwise trending toward restriction — the *Callais* gutting of VRA Section 2 ([[2026-04-29--scotus-callais-guts-section-2-vra-reworks-gingles-standard]]) and the Alabama-map shadow-docket order ([[2026-06-02--scotus-shadow-docket-reinstates-alabama-gop-map-over-intentional-discrimination-finding]]). It lands amid the administration's mail-voting pressure campaign — the [[2026-06-15--trump-dhs-1b-grant-threat-force-state-voting-overhaul-midterms]] grant threat and the Talwani EO challenge ([[2026-06-17--talwani-allows-mail-ballot-eo-challenge-rejects-ripeness-delay]]) — and may give states legal cover to resist phase-outs of mail voting. Barrett writing for the liberal bloc marks a notable limit on her deference to election-administration overreach.
