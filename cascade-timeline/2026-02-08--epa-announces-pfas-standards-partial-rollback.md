The Environmental Protection Agency moved forward with plans to rescind drinking water standards for four PFAS chemicals while extending compliance deadlines for two others, continuing a rollback of Biden-era public health protections. The EPA announced it will maintain Maximum Contaminant Levels for PFOA and PFOS established in April 2024, but extend compliance deadlines from 2029 to 2031, giving drinking water systems additional time to develop treatment plans. However, the agency announced its intent to rescind national drinking water standards for HFPO-DA (GenX), PFNA, PFHxS, and the hazard index used to evaluate combined risk of those compounds plus PFBS, stating it will reconsider the regulatory determinations to ensure they follow proper legal processes under the Safe Drinking Water Act.

In January 2026, the D.C. Circuit Court of Appeals denied EPA's request to summarily vacate the four contested PFAS drinking water rules, explaining that despite EPA's arguments about procedural inadequacies, the merits of the parties' positions were "not so clear as to warrant summary action." This court setback has not deterred the agency from proceeding with its rollback plans. EPA announced a proposed rule in Fall 2025 and plans to finalize changes extending PFOA and PFOS compliance to 2031 in Spring 2026, while simultaneously working to eliminate protections for the four other PFAS chemicals.

The partial rollback of PFAS standards represents a significant retreat from comprehensive protections against these persistent toxic chemicals, commonly called "forever chemicals" because they do not break down in the environment and accumulate in human bodies. PFAS exposure has been linked to cancer, immune system suppression, thyroid disease, and developmental problems. The original Biden-era rule establishing Maximum Contaminant Levels for six PFAS represented the first comprehensive federal drinking water standards for these widespread contaminants, affecting thousands of public water systems nationwide.

State governments have responded by accelerating their own PFAS regulations to fill the void left by federal retreat. Policy experts note that litigation and state-level action show no signs of slowing, with predictions that every state will eventually enact reporting obligations and product restrictions on PFAS. The EPA launched a "PFAS OUT" initiative to connect with public water utilities needing capital improvements to address PFAS, but critics argue this cannot substitute for enforceable federal standards. The rollback prioritizes industry interests over public health, leaving millions of Americans potentially exposed to dangerous levels of toxic chemicals in their drinking water while state-by-state approaches create inconsistent protections.
