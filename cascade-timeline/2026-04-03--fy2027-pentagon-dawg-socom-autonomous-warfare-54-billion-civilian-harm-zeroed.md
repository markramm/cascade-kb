The Trump administration's FY2027 Department of War budget request, released April 3, 2026 by the Office of Management and Budget, proposes $54.6 billion for the Defense Autonomous Warfare Group (DAWG) — a 24,000-percent increase from its inaugural-year FY2026 allocation of $225.9 million. The DAWG is housed within a subordinate command at U.S. Special Operations Command (SOCOM) and functions as the Pentagon's institutional engine for drone procurement and AI-enabled autonomous targeting. The budget's $1.5 trillion topline ($1.15 trillion discretionary plus $350 billion in mandatory reconciliation spending) represents the largest defense request since World War II. The same budget zeroed out funding for the Pentagon's Civilian Harm Mitigation and Response Action Plan (CHMR-AP) — the program created in 2022 to track and reduce civilian casualties in U.S. strikes. A subsequent DoD Inspector General report found the department had not implemented any CHMR-AP objectives by the end of FY2025. On April 29, 2026, Secretary Pete Hegseth told the House Armed Services Committee: "We will shortly announce a sub-unified command for autonomous warfare." On April 22, 2026, SOUTHCOM Commander Gen. Francis L. Donovan established the SOUTHCOM Autonomous Warfare Command (SAWC), the first theater-level autonomous warfare command, tasked with deploying aerial, surface, and underwater drones for counter-narcotics interdiction, maritime patrol, and kinetic strikes across Latin America and the Caribbean. (Note on naming: Ken Klippenstein's April 22, 2026 report refers to a "Special Operations Forces Autonomous Warfare Center" — defense-press coverage and official HASC testimony consistently use "Defense Autonomous Warfare Group (DAWG)" as the formal designation. Entry uses the confirmed name.)

The DAWG's proposed $54.6 billion dwarfs the entire FY2027 Marine Corps request ($52.8 billion) and is concentrated in the reconciliation package, giving it less congressional scrutiny than baseline appropriations — a structural feature that mirrors the oversight gap in other rapid-buildup programs (see: 2025-03-05--scale-ai-pentagon-thunderforge-military-planning and 2026-03-21--palantir-maven-program-of-record). DoD Directive 3000.09, the regulatory framework governing lethal autonomous weapons review, contains a waiver provision exercisable by the Deputy Secretary "in conditions of urgent military need," making the paper safeguard largely discretionary. The simultaneous elimination of civilian-harm mitigation accountability and the exponential funding of autonomous lethal systems is the operational signature of the four-needs-coalition lethal-control-vector: the state builds enemies (the drug war, Iran), the surveillance apparatus provides targeting infrastructure (Maven Smart System, Palantir — see 2026-03-18--democracy-now-ai-kill-chain-palantir-iran), and the holy warriors supply the normative license to kill at scale without accountability. Adm. Brad Cooper's CENTCOM assurance that "humans will always make final decisions on what to shoot and what not to shoot and when to shoot" — issued during the Iran war in which AI compressed multi-day targeting to seconds (Operation Epic Fury, February–March 2026) — marks the human-in-the-loop framing as a rhetorical holding position rather than an operational constraint. Gen. Stanley McChrystal, writing for the McChrystal Group in February 2024 and quoted by Klippenstein, invoked the Greek myth of the infant Hercules strangling snakes in his crib — framing lethal AI autonomy as foreordained and unconfinable: "AI will grow to be strong — and a part of almost everything we do going forward." The AI-governance pincer closes here: the state simultaneously uses export-control law to throttle commercial AI safety work (see: 2026-06-12--commerce-export-control-order-forces-anthropic-to-suspend-fable-5-mythos-5-worldwide) while removing the institutional accountability structures that would constrain autonomous lethal systems in the field.
