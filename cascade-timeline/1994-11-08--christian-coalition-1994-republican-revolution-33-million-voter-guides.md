In the week before the 1994 midterm elections, the Christian Coalition distributes 33 million voter guides — 10 million for primary races earlier in the cycle plus 17 million congressional scorecards in the final days — through hundreds of thousands of evangelical and Catholic churches across America. The $5 million voter-guide operation is the single most concentrated exercise of evangelical electoral infrastructure in American history to that point.

The results are unambiguous: Republicans gain 54 House seats and 8 Senate seats, taking control of both chambers for the first time since 1954. Newt Gingrich becomes Speaker of the House. University of Akron political scientist John Green, who studies evangelical voting patterns, finds that the Christian Coalition's church-based mobilization tips multiple close races to Republican candidates. The post-election consensus: evangelical turnout organized by the Christian Coalition elects more than half of the Republican freshman class.

**Ralph Reed's organizational achievement:** The Christian Coalition's voter-guide operation solves a distribution problem that every political campaign faces. Rather than building its own contact network, it parasitizes the existing infrastructure of 300,000+ American churches — their trust relationships, physical gathering spaces, and weekly congregational rhythms. A voter guide placed in a church bulletin on the Sunday before the election carries the implicit authority of the pastor's space; it is not experienced as campaign literature but as congregational information. The operation is technically "nonpartisan" — voter guides compare "candidate positions" on issues without explicit endorsement — but their design leaves no ambiguity about which candidates the church community should support.

**The Contract with America parallel:** In 1995, the Christian Coalition published its own "Contract with the American Family," a ten-point legislative agenda that extends the Gingrich Contract with America into social conservatism: a Religious Equality Amendment to the Constitution allowing school prayer, school choice vouchers, restrictions on pornography, abortion restrictions, and pro-family tax policy. The parallel naming is not accidental — Reed is signaling that the evangelical movement is a co-equal partner in the Republican majority, not a constituency to be managed.

By 1996, the Christian Coalition claims 1.9 million members, 2,000 local chapters, and a $27 million annual budget — built from $200,000 and 2,000 members in 1989 when Reed took over. The 1994 cycle represents the apex of the Reed-era Christian Coalition and the proof-of-concept for church-embedded electoral infrastructure that the Christian right will refine through the 2004 Ohio "Patriot Pastors" operation, the 2016 Trump megachurch tours, and the TPUSA Faith operation of 2023-2026.
