On May 11, 2026, President Trump publicly described the U.S.-Iran ceasefire as on "massive life support" after rejecting Tehran's most recent peace proposal — calling it "stupid" — because it omitted nuclear concessions and demanded sanctions relief, naval-blockade lifting, and compensation for war damage. Iran's parliament speaker responded that the military stands ready to "teach a lesson." Same day, Pentagon comptroller Jules Hurst informed House lawmakers that the U.S. war has now cost approximately $29 billion, revised up from a $25 billion late-April estimate. The war is in its 10th week; Brent crude trades at $105/barrel (up 44% from pre-war); U.S. gas at $4.06/gallon; April inflation hit 3.8%, the highest in three years.

The Iran war is operating as the cascade's wartime acceleration mechanism. Executive-power expansion runs faster during armed conflict; oversight institutions defer; defense-contractor procurement accelerates; opposition political speech (see Sen. Mark Kelly / Hegseth investigation, separate event) becomes investigable under classified-information frames. The $4B cost-overrun in three weeks demonstrates the apparatus's funding-cycle compounding. The "ceasefire on life support" framing — repeated as Trump declares it — operationalizes the administration's preference for unresolved conflict over resolved one: continued war preserves the wartime executive-power posture and the wartime defense-spending profile. Reframes earlier Powell-memo institutional-spine framework: wartime is when the apparatus most readily produces what Powell-era counter-mobilization was designed to enable.

**Coverage update (2026-06-05, digest H5)**: Congressional follow-up through June 3-5 window adds the fiscal-opacity dimension. At congressional hearings, Hegseth refused to specify when a supplemental funding request would be submitted, saying the department would share costs "when it's relevant and required." Rep. Betty McCollum (House Appropriations) ordered detailed cost breakdowns — covering munitions, personnel, facilities damage, fuel, and ship maintenance — to be submitted by June 11, 2026 (action-forcing deadline). This runs simultaneously with a $1.5 trillion base defense budget request, creating the fiscal-opacity dual-track pattern: an unquantified war cost escalation funded out of existing appropriations beside a record base budget request, obscuring the true war cost from public oversight. The June 11 McCollum deadline is the next war-military action-forcing event.
