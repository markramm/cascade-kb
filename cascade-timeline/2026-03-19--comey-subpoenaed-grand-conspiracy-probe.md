On March 19, 2026, former FBI Director James Comey was subpoenaed by federal prosecutors in the Southern District of Florida as part of a sprawling "grand conspiracy" investigation targeting Obama-era intelligence officials. The probe, led by Trump-appointed U.S. Attorney Jason A. Reding Quinones, also targeted former CIA Director John Brennan and former Special Counsel Jack Smith, representing the most aggressive use of federal prosecutorial power against former government officials in modern American history.

The investigation had issued more than 130 subpoenas, a staggering number that indicated the probe's extraordinary breadth. Prosecutors appeared to be constructing a theory that senior intelligence and law enforcement officials had engaged in a coordinated conspiracy related to the investigations of Russian interference in the 2016 election and subsequent actions during Trump's first term. Legal scholars noted that the probe effectively sought to criminalize official actions taken by career intelligence professionals.

The involvement of Judge Aileen Cannon in handling the grand jury proceedings drew immediate concern from legal observers. Cannon — who had previously intervened on Trump's behalf in the Mar-a-Lago classified documents case and was ultimately reversed by the Eleventh Circuit — was seen by critics as a jurist sympathetic to Trump's legal interests. Her role in overseeing the grand jury added to perceptions that the investigation was designed to produce politically favorable outcomes rather than pursue legitimate criminal conduct.

The scope and targets of the probe sent a chilling message through the intelligence community and federal law enforcement: officials who investigated or prosecuted Trump during his first term now faced the prospect of criminal prosecution themselves. Former officials and their lawyers characterized the investigation as an unprecedented weaponization of the justice system for political retribution, warning that it would deter future officials from conducting legitimate investigations of presidential misconduct.
