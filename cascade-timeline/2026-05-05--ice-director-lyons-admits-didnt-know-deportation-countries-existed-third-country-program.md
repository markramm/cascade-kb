Acting ICE Director Todd Lyons, speaking at a "State of the Border" panel discussion, openly admitted: "Now we are actually removing people to countries that I didn't even know existed." Lyons characterized the third-country deportation program as "a huge game changer" in implementing President Trump's mass-deportation agenda. Lyons announced his resignation on April 16, 2026, effective end of May; the panel comments were among his last public statements as acting director.

The admission documents — from the agency head himself, on the record — that ICE is removing human beings to destinations that the agency's own director cannot identify. Third-country removal under U.S. immigration law historically required diplomatic agreements specifying receiving-country obligations to the deportee (non-refoulement guarantees, processing protocols, due-process review). Lyons's statement is structurally significant for two reasons: first, it confirms that the third-country pipeline has expanded beyond the negotiated agreements that previously constrained it; second, it confirms that operational decision-making about destinations has been delegated below the director level, with the director providing nominal sign-off without geographic literacy. This pairs with the documented 17 ICE custody deaths in the first four months of 2026 (one death roughly every six days, see [[2026-03-17--ice-detention-expansion-bipartisan-resistance]]) to establish a pattern: the deportation apparatus is operating at a velocity that has outrun both its operational oversight (deaths) and its geographic competence (Lyons's admission). Lyons's resignation, announced three weeks before this admission, removes the only named-and-Senate-confirmable accountability surface for the program.
