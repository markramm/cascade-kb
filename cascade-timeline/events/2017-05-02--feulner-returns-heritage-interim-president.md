summary: 'Ed Feulner, who had retired as Heritage Foundation president in 2013 after 36 years of leadership, returned as interim president and CEO on May 2, 2017, after Heritage''s Board of Trustees unanimously forced out Jim DeMint due to "significant and worsening management issues" that had created internal turmoil and damaged the organization''s effectiveness. Feulner''s return demonstrated Heritage''s reliance on its founder''s institutional knowledge and leadership capacity to stabilize the organization during crisis, while also highlighting the challenges Heritage faced in transitioning from Feulner''s era of institution-building to a new generation of conservative leadership under Trump.


  Jim DeMint had succeeded Feulner as Heritage president on April 4, 2013, bringing a high political profile as a former U.S. Senator from South Carolina who had resigned from the Senate specifically to lead Heritage. DeMint had promised to carry on Feulner''s legacy and build on Heritage''s institutional success. However, his four-year tenure proved troubled, marked by internal conflicts between Heritage Foundation''s research and policy functions and Heritage Action''s more aggressive political advocacy, staff departures, and what the Board ultimately determined were failures of management and leadership that threatened Heritage''s institutional effectiveness.


  The Board''s May 2, 2017 statement announcing DeMint''s forced resignation was diplomatically worded but clear about fundamental problems: "The Heritage Foundation''s Board of Trustees, by a unanimous vote, asked for and received the resignation of Jim DeMint as President and CEO of the organization. The Board determined there were significant and worsening management issues that led to a breakdown of internal communications and cooperation. Jim DeMint and a handful of his closest advisers failed to resolve these problems." This public acknowledgment of management failures from an organization that prided itself on conservative competence and institutional effectiveness suggested serious internal dysfunction.


  Multiple reports indicated that DeMint had centralized decision-making among a small group of advisers rather than working through Heritage''s established institutional structures, alienating long-time Heritage staff and fellows who had thrived under Feulner''s more collaborative approach. DeMint''s leadership style reportedly created tensions between Heritage Foundation''s traditional policy research functions and Heritage Action''s more confrontational political advocacy, with some conservative donors and policymakers questioning whether Heritage had become too overtly partisan and lost effectiveness as a policy development organization.


  Staff resignations during DeMint''s tenure had been significant, with experienced policy analysts and researchers leaving Heritage for other conservative organizations or think tanks. This brain drain threatened Heritage''s institutional capacity and credibility, particularly as the Trump administration sought policy guidance and personnel recommendations. The Board''s decision to remove DeMint appeared to reflect concerns that continued management dysfunction would permanently damage Heritage''s position as the preeminent conservative policy organization.


  Feulner''s return as interim president brought immediate stability and credibility. At 75 years old, Feulner remained deeply respected within conservative movement circles and maintained relationships with major donors, conservative leaders, and Republican policymakers that had been built over four decades. His return signaled that Heritage would maintain institutional continuity and conservative movement priorities while searching for a permanent successor who could provide effective leadership for the Trump era and beyond.


  The timing of Feulner''s return proved consequential. The Trump administration, inaugurated just over three months before DeMint''s ouster, was still building its policy infrastructure and personnel. Heritage played a crucial role in staffing the Trump administration and developing policy frameworks across domestic and foreign policy domains. Feulner''s return ensured that Heritage could continue this supportive role effectively despite the leadership transition, maintaining Heritage''s traditional "government-in-waiting" function for Republican administrations.


  Feulner had already been serving on President Trump''s transition team following Trump''s November 2016 election victory, providing continuity between Heritage''s policy development work and Trump administration implementation. This transition role reflected Heritage''s established pattern of closely coordinating with incoming Republican administrations, a function that Feulner had pioneered with Reagan in 1981 and that Heritage had maintained through subsequent Republican presidencies. Feulner''s experience made him invaluable for ensuring Heritage continued supporting the Trump administration effectively.


  During his second tenure as president, though serving only as interim leader, Feulner oversaw the search for a permanent successor while managing Heritage''s day-to-day operations and maintaining the organization''s policy production, communications, and advocacy functions. His institutional knowledge and established relationships allowed Heritage to maintain effectiveness during what could have been a destabilizing leadership transition. The Board''s decision to bring Feulner back rather than appointing another interim leader demonstrated both Feulner''s unique status within Heritage and the organization''s recognition that foundational leadership skills were needed during crisis.


  Heritage''s Board ultimately selected Kay Coles James as DeMint''s permanent successor, with James assuming the presidency in January 2018. James, an African American conservative who had served in senior positions in the Reagan, George H.W. Bush, and George W. Bush administrations, brought both government experience and management skills that the Board viewed as essential for Heritage''s future. Feulner''s eight-month interim tenure had stabilized Heritage and positioned the organization for James''s leadership, which would focus on developing Project 2025—the comprehensive policy blueprint for a potential second Trump administration.


  Feulner''s willingness to return as interim president at age 75, despite having retired four years earlier, demonstrated his continuing commitment to Heritage and the conservative movement. His second tenure, though brief, proved essential to Heritage''s institutional continuity and effectiveness during a critical period when Republican control of the White House and Congress created opportunities for advancing conservative policy priorities. The episode also highlighted Feulner''s irreplaceability in certain respects—no one else combined his institutional knowledge, donor relationships, conservative movement networks, and leadership capacity in ways that could stabilize Heritage during crisis.


  The DeMint ouster and Feulner''s return illustrated broader challenges facing conservative institutions as they transitioned from the founders who had built them in the 1970s-1980s to new leadership for the 21st century. Heritage''s dependence on Feulner''s return during crisis suggested that institutional knowledge and relationships developed over decades remained difficult to transfer to new leaders, even those with strong conservative credentials and political profiles. The conservative infrastructure that Feulner and others had built over 40+ years had become complex ecosystems requiring sophisticated management and deep understanding of movement coordination.


  Feulner''s second tenure concluded in January 2018 when James assumed the presidency, but Feulner remained connected to Heritage as founder and continued participating in conservative movement activities. His two separate terms as Heritage president (1977-2013 and 2017-2018) totaled approximately 37 years of leadership, during which he transformed Heritage from a small startup into the central policy organization of American conservatism. The 2017 return demonstrated that even in retirement, Feulner remained essential to Heritage''s effectiveness and the broader conservative movement''s institutional capacity.

On May 2, 2017, the Heritage Foundation's Board of Trustees unanimously voted to oust president Jim DeMint, citing "significant and worsening management issues" that had created internal turmoil and damaged the organization's effectiveness. DeMint, who had resigned from the U.S. Senate in 2012 to lead Heritage, had centralized decision-making among a small group of advisers, alienating long-time staff and triggering significant turnover among experienced policy analysts and researchers. The board immediately called on founder Edwin Feulner — retired since 2013 after 36 years as president — to return as interim leader.

Feulner's return at age 75 demonstrated Heritage's reliance on his foundational institutional knowledge and donor relationships. As interim president, he stabilized operations and maintained Heritage's critical role in staffing and advising the Trump administration, which had been inaugurated just three months earlier. Feulner had already been serving on Trump's transition team, providing continuity in Heritage's traditional "government-in-waiting" function for Republican administrations.

Feulner served as interim president until January 2018, when Kay Coles James — a veteran of three Republican administrations — assumed the permanent role. James would go on to oversee the development of Project 2025, the comprehensive policy blueprint for a second Trump administration. The DeMint ouster and Feulner's return illustrated challenges facing conservative institutions in transitioning from their founding generation to new leadership while maintaining institutional effectiveness and donor confidence.
