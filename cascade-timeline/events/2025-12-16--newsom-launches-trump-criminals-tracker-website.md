title: California Governor Newsom Launches Official State Website Tracking Trump's "Top 10 Criminal Cronies" as Direct Political Confrontation Escalates
summary: 'California Governor Gavin Newsom launched a new state government website on December 16, 2025, titled "Trump''s Criminal Cronies" that catalogs the criminal records, corruption cases, and extremist ties of individuals that President Donald Trump has elevated, protected, pardoned, or surrounded himself with. The website, hosted at the official California government domain (gov.ca.gov/trumpcriminals), represents an unprecedented use of state resources to directly confront a sitting president''s ethics and associates, marking a significant escalation in the ongoing political conflict between California''s Democratic leadership and the Trump Administration. The rollout coincided strategically with the release of new California crime statistics showing continued drops in violent crime rates, creating a deliberate contrast between what Newsom characterized as California''s successful crime reduction efforts and Trump''s pattern of pardoning and associating with convicted criminals.


  The website features detailed profiles of what Newsom''s office describes as "Trump''s top 10 criminal convictions that were followed by pardons," spanning January 6 Capitol rioters, former politicians convicted of corruption, cryptocurrency executives convicted of money laundering, and business figures convicted of fraud and drug trafficking. Each profile includes the individual''s crimes, their sentences, the dates of their Trump pardons or commutations, and their connections to Trump through business relationships, political associations, or personal loyalty. The website prominently features Donald Trump himself at the top of the list, labeled as the "criminal in chief" due to his 34 felony convictions in New York state court for falsifying business records related to hush money payments.


  The featured criminals include several high-profile cases that illuminate the Trump Administration''s pattern of rewarding loyalty and punishing accountability. Changpeng Zhao, the CEO of Binance cryptocurrency exchange, appears on the list for pleading guilty to money laundering charges and allowing billions of dollars to flow to terrorists, cybercriminals, and child abusers through his platform. Zhao''s case connects directly to broader corruption concerns about Trump family cryptocurrency ventures documented in recent Congressional investigations. Ross Ulbricht, founder of the Silk Road dark web marketplace that facilitated more than $214 million in illegal drug sales, received a Trump pardon despite having been sentenced to life in prison without possibility of parole. Former Honduran President Juan Orlando Hernández, convicted of large-scale drug trafficking and accepting bribes from Mexican cartels, received a Trump pardon shortly after the Honduran government agreed to favorable
  terms for Trump Organization hotel developments in Honduras.


  Rod Blagojevich, the only Illinois governor to be impeached and removed from office, appears prominently on Newsom''s website for his conviction on corruption charges including attempting to sell Barack Obama''s vacant Senate seat to the highest bidder. Blagojevich received a Trump commutation in 2020 during Trump''s first term and subsequently became a vocal Trump supporter, appearing regularly on conservative media to attack Trump''s critics. Michele Fiore, suspended from her position as justice court judge in Nye County, Nevada, was convicted on one count of conspiracy to commit wire fraud and six counts of wire fraud for stealing money that donors intended for fallen police officers'' memorials and spending it on personal expenses. Despite these convictions, Trump publicly defended Fiore and suggested he might pardon her.


  Former Representative George Santos (R-NY) appears on the list for his convictions on wire fraud and identity theft charges. Trump commuted Santos''s sentence in November 2025, just weeks before Santos was scheduled to begin his prison term. Santos had been one of the most scandal-plagued members of Congress, fabricating virtually his entire resume including claims about his education, employment history, and family background, while stealing from campaign donors and using campaign funds for personal expenses including luxury goods and cosmetic procedures. Trump''s commutation of Santos''s sentence came after Santos appeared at Trump rallies and used his social media platform to defend Trump against criminal charges.


  The website also highlights several January 6 Capitol riot participants who received Trump pardons. These individuals were convicted of crimes ranging from assaulting police officers to seditious conspiracy related to the violent attack on the U.S. Capitol aimed at preventing the certification of the 2020 presidential election results. Trump issued blanket pardons for virtually all January 6 defendants shortly after beginning his second term, describing them as "patriots" who were "political prisoners." Newsom''s website features several of the most violent January 6 offenders, including individuals who assaulted Capitol Police officers with weapons, those who led organized groups that breached the Capitol building, and those who made explicit threats to kill members of Congress and Vice President Mike Pence.


  The timing of the website''s launch proved strategically significant. Governor Newsom announced the tracker on the same day that California released updated crime statistics showing that homicides, violent assaults, and property crimes continued to decline across the state. Newsom''s announcement explicitly connected these two narratives: "The contrast couldn''t be clearer: California is driving crime down—while Donald Trump pardons drug lords and welcomes criminals into positions of power." This framing attempted to counter Trump''s repeated attacks on California as a crime-ridden failed state, turning the crime narrative back on Trump by highlighting his personal associations with convicted criminals.


  The website''s design and presentation reflected careful attention to making complex criminal cases accessible to general audiences. Each profile includes a summary of the individual''s crimes written in plain language, photographs of the individuals, timelines showing their convictions and pardons, and sourcing to court documents, news articles, and official records. The website includes an interactive feature allowing users to filter the criminals by category (corruption, violent crimes, fraud, drug offenses, etc.) and to see the connections between various criminals and Trump''s business interests or political operations. The production quality suggests significant state resources were devoted to the project, raising questions about whether using government employees and taxpayer funds for what Republicans characterized as a political attack website was an appropriate use of state authority.


  Governor Newsom''s statement accompanying the launch positioned the website as a public service providing transparency about the President''s associations. "While President Trump attacks California daily and spreads lies about crime in our state, the facts tell a different story," Newsom stated. "California has driven violent crime to historic lows through evidence-based policies and professional law enforcement. Meanwhile, the President surrounds himself with convicted criminals, pardons drug traffickers and fraudsters, and appoints people to positions of power who have demonstrated contempt for the rule of law. The American people deserve to know who the President has chosen to elevate and protect."


  The website launch represented the latest escalation in an ongoing political confrontation between Governor Newsom and President Trump that has intensified throughout Trump''s second term. Trump has repeatedly threatened to cut federal funding to California, attacked Newsom personally on social media, and used federal law enforcement resources to investigate Newsom''s associates and supporters. Newsom has responded by positioning California as the center of "resistance" to Trump Administration policies, using state resources to challenge federal actions in court, and increasingly using state government platforms to directly attack Trump''s character and fitness for office.


  Republican critics immediately condemned the website as an inappropriate partisan attack using government resources. Republican National Committee spokesperson issued a statement calling the website "a disgraceful abuse of taxpayer resources to pursue Gavin Newsom''s presidential ambitions through personal attacks on the President." Conservative media figures accused Newsom of hypocrisy, noting that California has historically supported criminal justice reform policies including reduced sentences and expanded pardon authority for state inmates. However, Newsom''s office distinguished between pardons or commutations based on rehabilitation and justice concerns versus Trump''s pattern of pardoning associates, political supporters, and individuals whose cases Trump saw on television or heard about from friends.


  The website''s launch sparked immediate debate about the appropriate boundaries of government communications and political speech. Government websites are typically expected to provide factual information about public services, policies, and official government activities rather than political commentary about federal officials. However, California''s lawyers defended the website as factually accurate information about matters of legitimate public concern. Every individual listed on the website was in fact convicted of the crimes described, and Trump did in fact pardon or commute their sentences. The website''s editorial framing and characterization of these individuals as Trump''s "criminal cronies" involved judgment, but that judgment was based on documented facts about criminal convictions and presidential pardons.


  Legal scholars noted that while the website was unusual, it likely fell within constitutional boundaries of state government speech. The First Amendment generally permits government entities to engage in their own speech, even when that speech is controversial or politically charged. As long as California was not compelling private parties to endorse messages they disagree with or preventing dissenting viewpoints from being heard, the state''s creation of a website expressing the Governor''s views about federal officials was protected government speech. However, scholars noted that the use of government resources for what appeared primarily to be political messaging rather than governance raised ethical and political questions even if constitutional concerns were minimal.


  The website''s focus on cryptocurrency-related crimes proved particularly timely given the December 16 House Judiciary Committee hearing on Trump Administration corruption in antitrust enforcement and the recent release of House Democrats'' report on Trump family cryptocurrency ventures. The inclusion of Changpeng Zhao and other crypto industry figures on Newsom''s criminal cronies list connected directly to broader concerns about the Trump Administration''s pay-to-play approach to cryptocurrency regulation. Newsom''s team deliberately highlighted these connections, with the website including a section titled "Crypto Corruption" that detailed how several pardoned individuals had connections to cryptocurrency firms that invested in Trump family ventures or hired Trump-connected lobbyists.


  The website launch occurred against a backdrop of escalating federal-state conflict over law enforcement and criminal justice policy. The Trump Administration''s Department of Justice had threatened to prosecute California officials for state policies that limited cooperation with federal immigration enforcement. The Administration had also suggested investigating California''s prison release policies and threatened to condition federal law enforcement grants on California changing its criminal justice reform measures. Newsom''s criminal cronies website represented a rhetorical counterattack, shifting attention from California''s policies to Trump''s personal associations with criminals.


  Media coverage of the website was extensive but predictably divided along partisan lines. Liberal outlets portrayed the website as effective political communication that used factual information to highlight Trump''s hypocrisy on crime issues. Conservative outlets described it as a partisan stunt that wasted taxpayer money and degraded political discourse. Fact-checkers verified that the criminal convictions and pardons listed on the website were accurate, though they noted that the website''s characterizations and framing involved political judgment. Several mainstream outlets ran profiles of individuals featured on the website, providing detailed reporting on their crimes and their connections to Trump, effectively amplifying the website''s message.


  The website generated significant social media engagement, with the hashtag #TrumpCriminals trending nationally within hours of the launch. Supporters shared profiles of specific criminals featured on the site, while critics accused Newsom of hypocrisy and pointed to crimes committed by individuals associated with Democratic politicians. The controversy drove substantial traffic to California''s government website, with the criminal cronies page receiving millions of views in its first 24 hours online. This demonstrated the effectiveness of the website as a communication strategy, as it succeeded in focusing national attention on Trump''s pardons and criminal associations.


  Governor Newsom''s presidential ambitions provided an important context for the website launch. Newsom is widely viewed as a potential Democratic presidential candidate in 2028 or in the event that Trump becomes unable to serve. The criminal cronies website allowed Newsom to establish a national profile as a direct and effective Trump critic, demonstrating his willingness to confront the President aggressively rather than avoid conflict. The website''s professional presentation and strategic messaging suggested that Newsom''s team was thinking beyond California state politics to national political messaging and presidential campaign communications.


  The website included a feature allowing users to sign up for email updates, effectively creating a political email list using state government infrastructure. While the signup form claimed that emails would only provide updates about the website''s content, critics noted that this created a mechanism for Newsom to build a supporter email list at taxpayer expense. California''s lawyers defended the email feature as similar to email subscription options available on many government websites, allowing interested citizens to receive updates about matters of public concern. However, the political dimensions of this data collection raised ethical questions about the line between government communications and political campaign list-building.


  Several individuals featured on the website responded publicly to their inclusion. George Santos posted on social media that he was "honored to be included on Gavin Newsom''s enemies list" and that "being attacked by failed California proves I''m doing something right." Rod Blagojevich appeared on conservative media outlets to attack Newsom and defend his Trump pardon. Others remained silent, likely on advice of counsel given ongoing legal proceedings or civil litigation related to their convictions.


  The website''s long-term significance extends beyond its immediate political impact. By creating a comprehensive, well-documented catalog of Trump''s pardons and criminal associates, California established a resource that researchers, journalists, and future prosecutors could reference when examining Trump Administration corruption. The website''s documentation of the connections between pardoned individuals and Trump business interests or political operations provided a foundation for understanding the systematic nature of Trump''s approach to using presidential pardon power to reward loyalty and protect associates.


  The December 16, 2025 launch of California''s "Trump''s Criminal Cronies" website represented a significant escalation in state-level political resistance to the Trump Administration. By using official state government infrastructure to catalog and publicize Trump''s associations with convicted criminals, Governor Newsom transformed state government communications into a vehicle for direct political confrontation with a sitting president. The website''s careful documentation of criminal convictions, pardons, and Trump connections provided factual grounding for political attacks while raising questions about the appropriate use of government resources for political messaging. The launch''s strategic timing—coinciding with positive California crime statistics and Congressional hearings on Trump Administration corruption—demonstrated sophisticated political communications planning. Whether the website represented appropriate transparency or inappropriate partisan warfare depended largely
  on one''s political perspective, but its impact on focusing national attention on Trump''s pardons and criminal associates was undeniable.

On December 16, 2025, California Governor Gavin Newsom launched a state government website at gov.ca.gov/trumpcriminals cataloging the criminal records, corruption cases, and extremist ties of individuals that President Donald Trump had pardoned, elevated, or surrounded himself with. The website's launch was timed to coincide with the release of California crime statistics showing continued statewide drops in violent crime, including an 18% decline in homicides, creating a deliberate contrast between what Newsom characterized as California's successful public safety record and Trump's pattern of pardoning convicted criminals. The site featured what it described as "Trump's top 10 criminal convictions that were followed by pardons," prominently listing Trump himself — who held 34 felony convictions in New York for falsifying business records — as the "criminal in chief."

The website profiled a range of pardoned individuals whose cases illustrated different dimensions of Trump's use of presidential clemency. Changpeng Zhao, CEO of the Binance cryptocurrency exchange, had pleaded guilty to money laundering charges involving billions of dollars flowing to terrorists, cybercriminals, and child abusers. Ross Ulbricht, founder of the Silk Road dark web drug marketplace, had been sentenced to life in prison without parole. Former Honduran President Juan Orlando Hernández was convicted of large-scale drug trafficking and accepting bribes from Mexican cartels. Former Illinois Governor Rod Blagojevich had been convicted of attempting to sell Barack Obama's vacated Senate seat. Former Representative George Santos (R-NY) had been convicted of wire fraud and identity theft after fabricating much of his biography and stealing from campaign donors; Trump commuted Santos's sentence in November 2025, weeks before he was due to report to prison. The site also featured several January 6 Capitol riot participants, including individuals convicted of assaulting police officers and seditious conspiracy.

Governor Newsom stated at the launch: "California is driving crime down — while Donald Trump pardons drug lords and welcomes criminals into positions of power." Republican critics condemned the website as a partisan abuse of state government resources and taxpayer funds, with the Republican National Committee calling it "a disgraceful use of taxpayer resources to pursue Gavin Newsom's presidential ambitions." California's legal team defended the site as factually accurate public information — every conviction and pardon listed was documented in court records — while legal scholars noted that government speech doctrine likely insulated the site from First Amendment challenge even given its political framing. The website's launch drew national attention and generated significant social media engagement, with the hashtag #TrumpCriminals trending within hours, effectively amplifying the site's message about Trump's pardons and his associates' criminal records to a national audience well beyond California.
