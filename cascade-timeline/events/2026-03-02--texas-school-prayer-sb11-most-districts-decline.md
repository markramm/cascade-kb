Texas Senate Bill 11 required school boards to decide by March 1, 2026 whether to establish a daily devotional period during which students could pray, read the Bible, or engage in other religious activity. The bill passed in 2025 as part of the Texas Legislature's broader package of Christian nationalist educational mandates — which also included a requirement that every public school classroom display the Ten Commandments. Despite gubernatorial backing and intense pressure from the religious right, reporting from the Texas Tribune found that only approximately 15 school districts had opted into the prayer period, with the vast majority of urban, suburban, and rural districts voting against.

The opt-in structure required participating districts to obtain signed parental consent forms that waived parents' right to sue the district for Establishment Clause violations — a legally novel mechanism that civil rights attorneys argued was itself unconstitutional. More than 160 Texas faith leaders had urged districts to oppose the policy, citing administrative burden, existing student religious rights, and potential harm to non-participating students. The ACLU of Texas had published a detailed analysis arguing SB 11 violated the constitutional separation of church and state.

The near-universal non-adoption by school districts, even in deeply conservative Texas, revealed the gap between legislative Christian nationalist signaling and practical implementation. However, the law's passage — combined with the Ten Commandments classroom mandate and a concurrent state-developed curriculum rich in Christian references — established a legal and political template that other states were monitoring for adoption. The episode documents a pattern in which religious-right state capture produces symbolic legislation designed as much to litigate church-state boundaries upward to the Supreme Court as to change immediate practice.
