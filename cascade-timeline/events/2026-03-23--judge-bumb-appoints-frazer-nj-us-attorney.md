On March 23, 2026, Chief Judge Renee Marie Bumb of the District of New Jersey appointed career prosecutor Robert Frazer as the district's U.S. Attorney, resolving a months-long constitutional crisis that had paralyzed federal law enforcement in the state. Frazer, who had more than 20 years of experience as a federal prosecutor, was reportedly agreed upon by both the federal judges in the district and the Trump administration, marking a rare moment of cooperation after months of confrontation.

The appointment brought closure to a saga that had become a national symbol of the Trump administration's disregard for constitutional appointment requirements. The crisis began when Alina Habba, Trump's former personal lawyer, was installed as U.S. Attorney but subsequently found by courts to have been unlawfully appointed. Habba resigned in December 2025, but the leadership vacuum she left behind only deepened the crisis.

Her successors — Philip Lamparello, Jordan Fox, and Ari Fontecchio — were found unconstitutional on March 9 when courts ruled they too violated the Appointments Clause. The situation escalated dramatically on March 17, when Judge Zahid Quraishi ejected a DOJ prosecutor from his courtroom and demanded testimony about Habba's continuing involvement in office operations, declaring that a generation of prosecutors had destroyed the office's goodwill in a single year.

Frazer's appointment under the court's authority represented an extraordinary exercise of judicial power to ensure the continued functioning of the federal justice system when the executive branch had failed to provide lawful leadership. His career background and bipartisan acceptance signaled that both sides recognized the need to restore credibility to an office whose legitimacy had been called into question for months, potentially jeopardizing hundreds of pending federal prosecutions.
