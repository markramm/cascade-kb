As Operation Epic Fury launched on February 28, 2026, reporting and advocacy group statements documented the extent to which evangelical end-times theology — not solely geopolitical calculation — had shaped the decision to strike Iran. The convergence was visible across multiple layers of the administration's political ecosystem.

U.S. Ambassador to Israel Mike Huckabee, a former Baptist pastor, had been among the most explicit voices arguing that the Iran conflict carried biblical significance. Huckabee had previously suggested to Trump that he would "hear from heaven" when deciding whether to support Israeli military action against Iran, and publicly advanced the position that Israel possessed a God-given right to the land of the Middle East — remarks that provoked international outcry. Huckabee's role as ambassador placed an end-times theological worldview at the center of U.S.-Israel diplomatic communications in the period leading to the strikes.

Pastor John Hagee, founder and chairman of Christians United for Israel (CUFI), offered explicit biblical sanction for the strikes on Fox News in the days before the operation launched, arguing that Trump was "uniquely suited to pursue a biblically mandated policy of cutting down Israel's enemies." Hagee's premillennial dispensationalist theology — in which a climactic Middle East war triggers the Second Coming of Christ — is the dominant theological framework among the largest pro-Israel Christian organization in the United States, which claims more than 10 million members.

The Freedom From Religion Foundation issued a public statement on February 28 warning that "Christian nationalist End Times ideology is driving U.S. policy toward Iran," arguing that the decision to strike represented not a conventional strategic calculus but the implementation of an apocalyptic theological agenda through military force.

Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth, who had previously declared at the National Prayer Breakfast that "America was founded as a Christian nation" and that soldiers who die for their country "find eternal life," had institutionalized evangelical Christianity throughout the Pentagon's command culture through monthly prayer services and explicit religious messaging. Under his leadership, the department framing of military action as spiritually sanctioned had become routine.

The Freedom From Religion Foundation and the Military Religious Freedom Foundation both documented the pattern: the Iran war decision emerged from an administration where the highest-level officials — the President, the Secretary of Defense, the Ambassador to Israel — openly operated from a theological framework in which geopolitical events are prophetic fulfillments, and where military force against a nation with predominantly Muslim population was explicitly framed as a Christian obligation.
