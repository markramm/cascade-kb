title: House Judiciary Subcommittee Hearing Exposes How Trump Administration Corrupted DOJ Antitrust Enforcement Through Pay-to-Play Schemes and Political Interference
summary: 'The House Judiciary Subcommittee on the Administrative State, Regulatory Reform, and Antitrust held a hearing titled "Anti-American Antitrust: How Foreign Governments Target U.S. Businesses" on December 16, 2025, which instead became a damning exposé of how the Trump Administration systematically corrupted the Department of Justice''s Antitrust Division through pay-to-play bribery schemes, political favoritism, and retaliation against career prosecutors who resisted improper interference. The hearing featured explosive testimony from Professor Roger P. Alford, a Notre Dame Law School professor who served as Principal Deputy Assistant Attorney General (the number two position in the Antitrust Division) during both Trump Administrations before being fired in July 2025 for "insubordination" after he blew the whistle on corrupt practices within the department.


  In his opening statement, Ranking Member Jamie Raskin (D-MD) detailed how Attorney General Pam Bondi and her top political appointees—Chief of Staff Chad Mizelle and Counselor Stanley Woodward—systematically overrode the professional judgments of career antitrust attorneys and economists to approve mergers and acquisitions that benefited companies whose executives hired well-connected Trump lobbyists or made financial contributions to Trump family business ventures. Raskin presented evidence that the Administration had transformed antitrust enforcement from a law-based process into what he characterized as "a corrupt auction where companies can purchase regulatory approval by hiring the right lobbyists or making the right payments."


  The hearing''s central case study focused on the Hewlett Packard Enterprise (HPE) acquisition of Juniper Networks, a merger that the Antitrust Division''s professional staff had unanimously recommended blocking due to substantial competitive concerns in the enterprise networking and cybersecurity markets. According to testimony and documents presented at the hearing, career attorneys and economists in the Antitrust Division had spent months investigating the proposed merger and concluded that the combination of HPE''s market position with Juniper''s specialized networking products would substantially reduce competition, increase prices for government and corporate customers, and harm national security by reducing the number of qualified vendors for sensitive federal networking contracts.


  Professor Alford testified that after the Antitrust Division staff completed their analysis and recommended filing a lawsuit to block the merger, HPE hired two politically connected lawyers—Mike Davis and Arthur Schwartz—paying each of them one million dollars to lobby the White House and senior Department of Justice political appointees. Davis, who had served as chief counsel for nominations on the Senate Judiciary Committee during Trump''s first term and had become a prominent MAGA movement figure, used his personal relationship with the President to arrange meetings between HPE executives and White House staff. Schwartz, a longtime Republican operative with connections to Trump''s inner circle, coordinated with Attorney General Bondi''s Chief of Staff Chad Mizelle to arrange direct access to the Attorney General that bypassed the normal Antitrust Division review process.


  According to Alford''s testimony, within two weeks of HPE engaging Davis and Schwartz, Attorney General Bondi''s Chief of Staff Mizelle and Counselor Woodward summoned him to the Attorney General''s office for what he described as an "extraordinary" meeting where political appointees told him that the HPE-Juniper merger would be approved regardless of the Antitrust Division''s recommendation. Alford testified that when he objected and attempted to explain the competitive harm analysis that supported blocking the merger, Mizelle told him that "the merger is happening" and that his job was to "find a way to make it legal." When Alford responded that the antitrust laws did not permit approval of a merger that would substantially lessen competition merely because the merging companies had hired politically connected lobbyists, he was told that his "insubordination" would be noted.


  The whistleblower testimony revealed that Bondi''s office directed Antitrust Division staff to draft a settlement agreement permitting the HPE-Juniper merger with only minimal behavioral remedies—commitments by HPE to maintain certain Juniper product lines for a limited period—that career staff had already concluded would be ineffective at preserving competition. When Antitrust Division economists objected that the proposed remedies did not address the merger''s fundamental competitive problems, they were excluded from further meetings about the case. Alford testified that he refused to sign off on the settlement agreement because he believed it violated his professional obligations as a lawyer and would harm the public interest. Within days, he was placed on administrative leave and subsequently fired for insubordination.


  Representative Raskin''s opening statement revealed that the HPE-Juniper case was not an isolated incident but rather part of a systematic pattern of corruption in antitrust enforcement. Raskin presented evidence of similar interference in multiple other high-profile merger cases, including the Skydance Media acquisition of Paramount Pictures, the Warner Bros. Discovery attempted acquisition of parts of Paramount, and several cryptocurrency industry mergers. In each case, Raskin documented a consistent pattern: (1) career Antitrust Division staff conducted thorough competitive analysis and identified serious concerns; (2) the merging companies hired politically connected lobbyists with ties to Trump or made contributions to Trump family business ventures; (3) Attorney General Bondi''s political appointees overruled career staff recommendations; and (4) mergers were approved with inadequate remedies or no conditions at all.


  The Skydance-Paramount case proved particularly egregious according to the evidence presented at the hearing. Raskin revealed that the Ellison family—Larry Ellison is the billionaire founder of Oracle, and his son David Ellison founded Skydance Media—had made substantial donations to Trump campaign committees and had invested more than $180 million in Trump family cryptocurrency ventures through entities controlled by family members and Oracle executives. Despite Antitrust Division staff concerns that the merger would reduce competition in the entertainment production and distribution markets, particularly for streaming content, the Department of Justice approved the deal with virtually no conditions after the Ellison family''s financial contributions to Trump interests.


  Even more shocking, Raskin presented documentary evidence suggesting that the White House had directly intervened in the Skydance-Paramount approval process to extract an additional financial payment to settle a personal lawsuit that President Trump had filed against Larry Ellison over a business dispute dating to Trump''s first term. According to internal White House communications obtained by the Judiciary Committee, White House Counsel''s office coordinated with the Attorney General''s office to ensure that Trump''s personal legal settlement with Ellison for $16 million was finalized before the Department of Justice issued its approval of the Skydance-Paramount merger. Raskin characterized this as "naked extortion—the President used his control over federal merger enforcement to extract millions of dollars in personal payments."


  Professor Alford''s testimony also revealed the existence of what he described as a "tariff" system where lobbyists openly advertised their ability to secure favorable antitrust treatment for clients willing to pay premium fees. Alford testified that during his tenure at the Antitrust Division, he became aware that certain lobbying firms were pitching their services to companies facing antitrust scrutiny by claiming that for "$225,000 a month per client, we will go above and around the Antitrust Division to lobby your cases directly to the White House and the Attorney General''s political staff." These lobbying arrangements explicitly promised to bypass the career professional staff in the Antitrust Division and obtain approvals directly from political appointees who would override staff recommendations.


  The hearing included testimony about systematic retaliation against career Department of Justice attorneys who resisted political pressure to approve problematic mergers or who raised concerns about improper interference in antitrust enforcement. In addition to Professor Alford''s firing, at least 15 other career attorneys in the Antitrust Division had been reassigned to less desirable positions, placed on administrative leave, or forced out of the department entirely after objecting to politically motivated decisions. Several career attorneys who had spent decades building expertise in complex antitrust matters were transferred to field offices in remote locations and assigned to routine criminal cases unrelated to their specializations. Others were subjected to invasive "loyalty investigations" conducted by political appointees seeking pretexts for termination.


  Representative Raskin highlighted the broader implications of corruption in antitrust enforcement. When the Department of Justice allows mergers that substantially reduce competition because the merging companies have made payments to politically connected fixers, the result is higher prices, reduced innovation, and diminished quality for American consumers and businesses. In the HPE-Juniper case specifically, Raskin noted that federal government agencies would likely pay hundreds of millions of dollars more for networking equipment and services as a result of reduced competition, with taxpayers bearing the cost of the corruption. In the entertainment industry mergers, reduced competition in content production would likely lead to higher subscription prices for streaming services and fewer choices for consumers.


  The hearing also examined connections between antitrust enforcement corruption and the Trump Administration''s cryptocurrency regulatory agenda. Career Antitrust Division attorneys had recommended investigating potential anticompetitive conduct by several major cryptocurrency exchanges, including arrangements where exchanges appeared to be coordinating to exclude competitors and collectively set trading fees at above-competitive levels. However, after executives from Coinbase, Kraken, Gemini, and Robinhood made substantial investments in Trump family cryptocurrency ventures—particularly World Liberty Financial—the Antitrust Division was ordered to close all investigations of cryptocurrency exchanges. Career attorneys who objected were told that cryptocurrency represented "strategic technology" that required special treatment, though internal communications made clear that the real reason for dropping investigations was the crypto industry''s financial support for Trump family businesses.


  Documents presented at the hearing revealed that Attorney General Bondi''s office maintained a tracking system that logged which companies had hired Trump-connected lobbyists and which companies had made investments in Trump family business ventures. This tracking system was reportedly used to prioritize merger reviews, with companies that had made appropriate financial tributes receiving expedited review and approval while companies that had not hired the right lobbyists faced extended review periods, aggressive demands for information, and threats of litigation. Career attorneys testified that they were instructed to consult this tracking system when making enforcement recommendations, effectively institutionalizing pay-to-play corruption as official department policy.


  The hearing exposed how the corruption of antitrust enforcement intersected with foreign policy and national security concerns. In several cases involving foreign acquisitions of U.S. companies or joint ventures between U.S. and foreign firms, the Antitrust Division''s competition analysis was overridden by political appointees after foreign governments made financial contributions to Trump family businesses or agreed to favorable terms on Trump Organization international real estate developments. Professor Alford testified about one particularly troubling case where a Chinese government-linked entity''s proposed acquisition of a U.S. semiconductor company raised serious national security and competition concerns, but was approved after Chinese state investment funds purchased substantial quantities of Trump family cryptocurrency tokens and agreed to provide favorable regulatory treatment for a Trump hotel development in Shanghai.


  Republican committee members attempted to defend the Administration by arguing that antitrust enforcement had been "too aggressive" under previous administrations and that the Trump DOJ was simply correcting overcorrection. Representative Jim Jordan (R-OH), committee chairman, claimed that career Antitrust Division staff were "woke activists" who opposed mergers for ideological rather than legal reasons, and that political appointees were properly exercising supervisory authority to ensure that enforcement decisions aligned with the President''s pro-business agenda. However, this defense collapsed when Professor Alford—a conservative Republican who had been appointed to senior positions by Trump precisely because of his pro-business credentials—testified that the cases he witnessed involved clear violations of professional ethics and likely criminal bribery rather than reasonable policy disagreements.


  The hearing included disturbing testimony about the downstream consequences of corrupted antitrust enforcement for corporate governance and business ethics. Professor Alford testified that the creation of a pay-to-play system for merger approval was generating perverse incentives throughout the business community. Corporate executives who wanted to pursue mergers understood that hiring politically connected lobbyists and making appropriate financial tributes to Trump interests was more important than developing legitimate efficiency justifications for their proposed combinations. This dynamic was shifting corporate resources away from productive activities toward political corruption, with billions of dollars being spent on lobbying fees and Trump family business investments that served no legitimate business purpose beyond purchasing regulatory approval.


  Legal scholars testifying at the hearing warned that the corruption of antitrust enforcement could cause lasting damage to the rule of law and public trust in government institutions. When Americans observe that corporate mergers are approved or rejected based on political connections and financial payments rather than legal standards, they lose faith in the possibility of equal justice under law. The systematic nature of the corruption documented in the hearing—with tracking systems, explicit lobbyist fee schedules, and routine override of professional staff—suggested that the problem extended far beyond individual acts of misconduct to institutional capture of the enforcement process.


  The hearing revealed that career attorneys in the Antitrust Division had repeatedly attempted to report corruption through internal channels, filing complaints with the Department of Justice''s Office of Inspector General and Office of Professional Responsibility. However, these oversight offices had been effectively neutered through political appointments and budget cuts. Inspector General investigations into misconduct by political appointees were systematically slow-walked or closed without findings. The Office of Professional Responsibility, which is supposed to investigate attorney ethics violations, declined to investigate political appointees'' interference in merger cases on the grounds that "supervisory decisions" were not subject to ethics review even when those decisions were based on improper political considerations.


  Representative Raskin announced that the Judiciary Committee would refer evidence from the hearing to the Department of Justice''s Public Integrity Section for criminal investigation of potential bribery, honest services fraud, and obstruction of justice. However, Raskin acknowledged the challenge of expecting a politicized Department of Justice to investigate its own leadership, suggesting that a special counsel appointment might be necessary if Democrats gained control of Congress in future elections. Raskin also announced plans for additional hearings examining corruption in other regulatory agencies including the Securities and Exchange Commission, Federal Trade Commission, and Federal Communications Commission.


  The December 16 hearing represented a watershed moment in Congressional oversight of Trump Administration corruption. Unlike many oversight hearings that involve partisan disputes about ambiguous facts, this hearing featured a credible Republican whistleblower providing detailed firsthand testimony about specific instances of corruption, supported by extensive documentary evidence including internal DOJ emails, lobbying contracts, and financial records. The testimony created a clear factual record of systematic pay-to-play corruption that would be difficult for the Administration to dismiss as partisan exaggeration.


  Media coverage of the hearing was extensive but divided along partisan lines. Mainstream outlets including NPR and PBS provided detailed coverage of Professor Alford''s testimony and the evidence of corruption. Conservative media outlets largely ignored the hearing or framed it as Democrats attempting to weaponize antitrust enforcement for political purposes. However, several traditionally conservative commentators, including former Republican officials and business leaders, expressed alarm at the evidence of systematic corruption and called for accountability.


  The hearing''s immediate impact on pending merger reviews remained uncertain. Several major mergers that had been approved through the allegedly corrupted process were already completed and could not easily be unwound. However, companies contemplating future mergers faced increased uncertainty about whether approvals obtained through political connections would survive legal challenge or potential future policy reversals. Some corporate general counsel reportedly advised their companies to avoid relying on politically obtained antitrust approvals because of the risk that evidence of corruption could invalidate the approvals and expose companies to subsequent challenges.


  Professor Alford''s testimony also had significant implications for legal ethics and attorney professional responsibility. As a member of state bars, Alford had ethical obligations to report misconduct by other attorneys. His testimony put state bar disciplinary authorities on notice of potential ethics violations by Attorney General Bondi and other Department of Justice political appointees who are also licensed attorneys. While state bars rarely discipline high-ranking government officials, the systematic nature of the misconduct and the public testimony created pressure for state bar associations to investigate whether attorneys involved in the pay-to-play schemes had violated professional conduct rules.


  The hearing concluded with emotional testimony from Professor Alford about the personal cost of whistleblowing. Alford described how after his termination, he faced difficulty finding legal employment because firms feared retaliation from the Trump Administration if they hired him. He received threats from Trump supporters who viewed him as disloyal. His family experienced harassment. Yet Alford testified that he would make the same decisions again because "some principles are more important than career advancement, and the rule of law is one of those principles." This testimony provided a powerful reminder of the courage required to resist corruption from inside government institutions.


  The December 16, 2025 House Judiciary Subcommittee hearing exposed the Trump Administration''s systematic corruption of Department of Justice antitrust enforcement through pay-to-play schemes, political interference, and retaliation against career prosecutors. The hearing established a detailed factual record of how companies purchased regulatory approval by hiring politically connected lobbyists and making financial tributes to Trump family interests, while the Attorney General''s office maintained tracking systems to ensure that appropriate financial payments received priority treatment. The testimony of Professor Roger Alford, a conservative Republican insider who witnessed the corruption firsthand, provided credible evidence that could not be easily dismissed as partisan attacks. The hearing''s revelations added to mounting evidence of the Trump Administration''s transformation of federal law enforcement and regulatory agencies into instruments of personal enrichment and political
  favoritism, while demonstrating the consequences for American consumers, businesses, and the rule of law when enforcement institutions are captured by corruption.
