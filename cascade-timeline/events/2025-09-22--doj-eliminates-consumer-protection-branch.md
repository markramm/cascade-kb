On September 22, 2025, the U.S. Department of Justice formally dissolved the Consumer Protection Branch (CPB), one of the primary federal enforcement arms protecting Americans from corporate fraud, pharmaceutical misconduct, and consumer safety violations. The elimination was part of a broader restructuring of the DOJ's Civil Division that effectively dismantled the institutional infrastructure built over decades to hold corporations accountable to federal law.

The Consumer Protection Branch had been responsible for enforcing the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetics Act, the Controlled Substances Act, the Consumer Product Safety Act, and dozens of related statutes covering pharmaceutical fraud, food safety, medical device misconduct, and deceptive trade practices. Its dissolution leaves enforcement fragmented between two new units without dedicated consumer fraud mission or institutional memory.

Under the restructuring, civil enforcement functions were moved to a newly created Enforcement and Affirmative Litigation Branch within the Civil Division — a unit that legal observers noted has redirected its activity toward cases aligned with the administration's political priorities, including immigration enforcement and challenges to state laws, rather than corporate accountability. Criminal consumer fraud cases were routed to a new Health and Safety Unit within the Criminal Division's Fraud Section.

Simultaneously, approximately three-quarters of career attorneys in the DOJ Civil Rights Division resigned or were pushed out through a combination of mass firings, reassignments, and hostile working conditions following the administration's takeover in January 2025. The hollowing out of the Civil Rights Division effectively ended enforcement of voting rights, employment discrimination, housing discrimination, and police reform agreements in the vast majority of pending cases.

Legal commentators widely characterized the restructuring as a deliberate dismantling of institutional consumer and civil rights protection, replacing agencies that policed corporate and government misconduct with units oriented toward advancing the administration's preferred enforcement targets. The effective end of federal consumer protection enforcement at the DOJ created a vacuum that state attorneys general — particularly in Democratic-led states — scrambled to fill, even as the administration simultaneously moved to preempt state-level enforcement through litigation and regulatory action.
