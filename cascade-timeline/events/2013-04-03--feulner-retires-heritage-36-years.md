summary: 'Edwin J. Feulner Jr. retired as president of the Heritage Foundation on April 3, 2013, after an extraordinary 36-year tenure that transformed Heritage from a small Capitol Hill operation with 9 staff members and a $1 million budget into the most influential conservative think tank in Washington, with over 240 staff members and annual revenue approaching $80 million. Feulner''s retirement marked the end of an era in which one individual''s vision and leadership built the intellectual and policy infrastructure that enabled the conservative movement to translate electoral victories into systematic policy implementation across federal, state, and local governments.


  When Feulner assumed Heritage''s presidency in 1977, the organization operated out of a rented office with minimal staff and resources. By the time of his retirement in 2013, Heritage had become what Newt Gingrich described as "the Parthenon of the conservative metropolis"—the central institution in an ecosystem of conservative think tanks, advocacy organizations, and donor networks that collectively constituted a shadow government capable of staffing Republican administrations and developing comprehensive policy agendas. Feulner''s achievement lay not merely in building one successful organization, but in demonstrating a replicable model for conservative institution-building that would be copied at state and local levels through the State Policy Network and other coordinating mechanisms.


  Under Feulner''s leadership, Heritage produced policy blueprints that shaped four Republican administrations. The 1981 "Mandate for Leadership" provided the Reagan administration with over 2,000 specific policy recommendations, approximately 60% of which were implemented during Reagan''s first term. Heritage produced subsequent Mandate editions for the George H.W. Bush administration (1989), the George W. Bush administration (2000), and would eventually develop Project 2025 for a potential second Trump administration. This "government-in-waiting" function—developing comprehensive policy agendas during Democratic administrations so that incoming Republican presidents could immediately begin implementing conservative priorities—represented one of Feulner''s most significant strategic innovations.


  Feulner''s success at Heritage depended critically on cultivating major donors and building sustainable funding streams. Under his leadership, Heritage secured massive financial support from Richard Mellon Scaife, who contributed more than $23 million to Heritage between 1975 and 1998, and served as vice-chairman of Heritage''s board of trustees. Scaife''s funding—described by Attorney General Edwin Meese as the "unseen hand" of the conservative movement—provided Heritage with financial stability that allowed long-term planning and institution-building. Beyond Scaife, Feulner expanded Heritage''s donor base to include approximately 600,000 individual contributors by 2013, creating a broad funding foundation that reduced dependence on any single donor.


  The policy infrastructure Feulner built at Heritage operated on multiple levels simultaneously. Heritage produced scholarly research for academic and intellectual credibility, rapid-response policy briefs for congressional offices and executive agencies, popular publications and media commentary to shape public opinion, and leadership training programs to develop the next generation of conservative policy professionals. This multi-layered approach ensured that Heritage''s influence extended beyond Washington to state legislatures, local governments, universities, and public discourse more broadly.


  Feulner personally maintained close relationships with conservative movement leaders, coordinating Heritage''s activities with other organizations including the Federalist Society, the American Legislative Exchange Council (which Feulner had helped co-found with Paul Weyrich in the early 1970s), the Council for National Policy, and numerous state-level think tanks. These coordination efforts ensured that conservative infrastructure worked synergistically rather than at cross-purposes, multiplying the movement''s effectiveness and preventing wasteful duplication of effort.


  One of Feulner''s most consequential legacies involved the talent pipeline he built between Heritage and Republican administrations. Heritage fellows and analysts regularly transitioned into senior government positions during Republican presidencies, then returned to Heritage during Democratic administrations to develop the next policy agenda. This revolving door created deep institutional knowledge about how to effectively implement conservative priorities once in power, addressing one of the key weaknesses the Powell Memorandum had identified in the early 1970s—that conservatives won elections but lacked the policy infrastructure to govern effectively according to movement principles.


  Heritage''s growth under Feulner''s leadership followed a deliberate trajectory. The budget grew from $1 million in 1977 to $2.5 million within 18 months, then to $14 million by 1987, and ultimately to approximately $80 million by 2013. Staff expanded from 9 in 1977 to over 240 by 2013. Heritage''s physical footprint grew from a rented Capitol Hill office to a substantial headquarters building. Perhaps most importantly, Heritage''s policy reach expanded from primarily domestic issues to include foreign policy, national security, constitutional interpretation, and eventually cultural and social issues that helped bind the religious right to the conservative economic agenda.


  The Heritage model pioneered by Feulner would be replicated through the State Policy Network (SPN), a coordination hub for conservative think tanks in all 50 states. Many of these state-level Heritage replicas received funding from the same donors who supported Heritage nationally, particularly the Scaife Foundations, creating a vertically integrated policy infrastructure that could promote coordinated conservative priorities from Washington down to state capitals and local governments. This state-level infrastructure proved essential for implementing conservative policies even during periods of Democratic control of federal government.


  Feulner''s retirement statement in April 2013 emphasized his pride in building an institution that would outlast his personal involvement. He remained with Heritage as Chancellor and chairman of the Asian Studies Center, and would later serve on President Trump''s transition team in 2016, ensuring that Heritage''s influence continued into a new administration. The Heritage Foundation board selected Senator Jim DeMint (R-SC), who had resigned from the Senate in January 2013, to succeed Feulner as president effective April 4, 2013. DeMint paid tribute to Feulner, saying he was "humbled to follow in the footsteps of Ed Feulner, who built the most important conservative institution in the nation."


  Feulner''s 36-year tenure at Heritage represented one of the most successful institution-building projects in modern American political history. By creating a professionally staffed, well-funded, ideologically focused organization capable of developing comprehensive policy agendas and supplying trained personnel to implement them, Feulner translated the aspirational vision of the Powell Memorandum into concrete reality. The infrastructure he built enabled systematic capture of regulatory agencies, courts, and policy-making processes across multiple levels of government—not through conspiracy or illegality, but through patient, strategic investment in the intellectual and organizational capacity to govern according to conservative movement priorities.


  The Heritage Foundation that Feulner handed to his successor in 2013 stood at the center of a vast ecosystem of conservative organizations, donors, media outlets, and political networks—an ecosystem that would prove instrumental in the Trump administration''s rapid implementation of conservative priorities and the development of Project 2025''s blueprint for systematic transformation of the federal government. Feulner''s retirement marked not an ending but a transition, as the infrastructure he built continued operating and expanding according to the principles and strategies he had pioneered over 36 years of movement leadership.

Edwin J. Feulner Jr. retired as president of the Heritage Foundation on April 3, 2013, after a 36-year tenure that transformed Heritage from 9 staff members with a $1 million budget into a 240-person organization with annual revenue approaching $80 million. When Feulner assumed the presidency in 1977, Heritage operated out of a rented Capitol Hill office. By his retirement, it had become what Newt Gingrich called "the Parthenon of the conservative metropolis" — the central institution in an ecosystem of conservative think tanks, advocacy groups, and donor networks capable of staffing Republican administrations and developing comprehensive policy agendas.

Under Feulner's leadership, Heritage produced policy blueprints that shaped four Republican administrations. The 1981 "Mandate for Leadership" provided the Reagan administration with over 2,000 specific recommendations, approximately 60 percent implemented in Reagan's first term. Subsequent Mandate editions were produced for the George H.W. Bush and George W. Bush administrations, with Project 2025 eventually developed for a potential Trump second term. Feulner also helped establish the State Policy Network, replicating the Heritage model at the state level through conservative think tanks in all 50 states, and cultivated a talent pipeline moving analysts between Heritage and Republican administrations through a revolving door that translated movement priorities into government action.

The Heritage Foundation board selected Senator Jim DeMint (R-SC), who had resigned his Senate seat in January 2013, to succeed Feulner as president effective April 4, 2013. Feulner remained with Heritage as Chancellor. He later served on President Trump's 2016 transition team, ensuring Heritage's institutional continuity into a new administration. Feulner's retirement marked not an ending but a transition: the infrastructure he built continued expanding, culminating in Heritage's most ambitious project — providing the policy blueprint for a systematic transformation of the federal government under a second Trump term.
