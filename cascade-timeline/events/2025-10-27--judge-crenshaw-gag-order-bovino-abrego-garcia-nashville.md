On October 27, 2025, U.S. District Judge Waverly Crenshaw Jr. in Nashville, Tennessee — presiding over the criminal human-trafficking case against Kilmar Abrego Garcia — issued a ruling directly rebuking Trump administration officials for extrajudicial statements that threatened Abrego Garcia's right to a fair trial. Crenshaw stopped short of a formal named gag order but directed prosecutors to formally notify DOJ and DHS employees — including DHS Secretary Noem and Attorney General Bondi — of the local rule prohibiting public statements with "a substantial likelihood of materially prejudicing" ongoing criminal proceedings.

The ruling targeted, without using his name, Greg Bovino, who had appeared on Fox News and Newsmax calling Abrego Garcia "an alien smuggler," "a wife beater," and "an MS-13 member," and describing the judges presiding over Abrego Garcia's civil and criminal cases as "extremist" and "activist." Crenshaw found these statements "troubling" and a violation of the constitutional norm of fair-trial rights. In December 2025, Abrego Garcia's attorneys escalated, asking Crenshaw to sanction the government after DHS Assistant Secretary Tricia McLaughlin amplified similar statements on X — with Bovino appearing again on conservative television using the same disqualifying language. DOJ argued that any sanction would amount to an unconstitutional "muzzle" on the executive branch.

The Crenshaw confrontation sits within the four-judges pattern as the case most directly involving Bovino's own extrajudicial conduct rather than the field conduct of agents he commands. Where Thurston confronted Bovino's enforcement methodology, Crenshaw confronted Bovino's media strategy — the simultaneous use of conservative media to poison a federal criminal jury pool while undermining judicial authority in real time. The Chicago Sun-Times documented both the Tennessee and Chicago courtroom confrontations in a January 2026 piece tracking Bovino's accumulating judicial conflicts.
