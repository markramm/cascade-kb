title: Fired Inspectors General Testify to Congress That Trump Administration Dismantled
  Federal Oversight Ecosystem
---
type: timeline_event

On February 25, 2026, a former Inspector General for the U.S. Office of Personnel Management testified before a congressional committee that the Trump administration had systematically dismantled the institutional infrastructure of federal oversight — firing dozens of IGs without legally required notice, cutting off funding for watchdog offices, disabling whistleblower hotlines, and embedding DOGE personnel inside agencies to demand access to sensitive data while circumventing normal oversight channels.

The witness, who was removed from their position on January 24, 2025 along with 16 fellow IGs in a late-night mass firing, told the committee that their removal occurred "with no notice... nor rationale for those decisions" — a direct violation of reforms to the Inspector General Act that require a substantive, case-specific rationale provided 30 days in advance of removal. The mass firing swept IGs from the Departments of Defense, State, Transportation, and Housing and Urban Development, as well as USAID, the Export-Import Bank, the Federal Housing Finance Agency, and other agencies.

The witness characterized the administration's actions as having "attempted to dismantle the entire ecosystem of oversight that protects against fraud, corruption, and cronyism in the federal government." Specific harms documented in the testimony included: interruption of a whistleblower hotline that had been responsible for identifying over $300 million in potential OPM savings; suspension of routine ethics and oversight trainings; the removal of the independent head of the Office of Special Counsel; and the September 2025 cutoff of funding for "SIGI" — the Special Inspector General infrastructure — by the White House, leaving multiple IG websites inaccessible.

The testimony described DOGE as an instrument of this oversight dismantling: "DOGE personnel demanded access to sensitive data related to federal employees" inside OPM, the witness said, with DOGE operating outside the normal channels through which independent oversight officials would have monitored and checked such access.

The mass IG firings were the subject of an investigation by American Oversight and a legal brief by the New York City Bar Association declaring the firings "illegal and invalid." The testimony before Congress marked the first formal on-record account from a fired IG describing the operational consequences of the administration's destruction of the federal watchdog system.
