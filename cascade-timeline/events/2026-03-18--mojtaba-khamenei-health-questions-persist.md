As of March 18, 2026 — ten days after the Assembly of Experts selected Mojtaba Khamenei as Iran's new Supreme Leader following his father Ayatollah Ali Khamenei's death in the opening strikes of the war — the new leader had not appeared on video or in public. All of his statements had been conveyed in writing or read by state television anchors over still photographs. The absence fueled growing speculation about his health and physical condition.

Questions centered on whether Mojtaba Khamenei had been injured in the same strikes that killed his father on or around February 28. The senior Khamenei's death had been confirmed by Iranian state media on March 8, but details about the circumstances — including whether other members of the supreme leader's inner circle were present — remained tightly controlled. Intelligence analysts noted that the new supreme leader's failure to appear even in a pre-recorded video was highly unusual for a leader seeking to consolidate authority during wartime.

Iranian state media maintained that Mojtaba Khamenei was actively directing the war effort from a secure location, characterizing his absence from public view as a standard security precaution during an active military conflict. State outlets published photographs of the new leader, but skeptics noted these could have been taken at any time and did not constitute proof of his current condition.

The uncertainty about the supreme leader's health had both domestic and strategic implications. Within Iran, the legitimacy of the succession process — already controversial given the dynastic nature of son succeeding father — depended in part on the new leader's ability to present himself as a commanding figure during the nation's gravest crisis since the 1980-88 war with Iraq. Internationally, the question of who was actually directing Iran's war strategy and who had authority to negotiate a ceasefire added another layer of complexity to an already opaque decision-making structure.
