Deputy Attorney General Todd Blanche delivered a chilling warning to corporate defense lawyers at the American Conference Institute's 42nd Annual Conference on Foreign Corrupt Practices Act (FCPA) and Global Anti-Corruption in Washington, DC. Speaking to hundreds of white-collar defense attorneys, Blanche explicitly stated that the Department of Justice monitors lawyers' public commentary and notices when their statements criticize the administration's enforcement priorities. He warned: 'If folks in this room are going to be honest brokers when counseling clients, posting on LinkedIn or writing client alerts, the public narrative should match the private one. If you publicly claim we are not enforcing white-collar crime aggressively enough, but privately insist that your clients are the victims of overreach, we notice that inconsistency.' Blanche also stated: 'It's remarkable how some members of the white-collar bar seem to have an endless stream of clients who are each coincidentally victims of supposed overreach or weaponization, but still publicly draft client alerts suggesting that the department is not prioritizing white-collar cases.' While he did not specify explicit repercussions, the implication was clear: lawyers who publicly criticize DOJ enforcement decisions may face disadvantages when seeking lenient treatment for their corporate clients.

The remarks represent an unprecedented threat from the nation's second-highest law enforcement official against lawyers exercising their First Amendment rights to critique government policy. Legal commentators characterized the statements as 'uncomfortable and clarifying,' noting that for the lawyers in the room, 'the Administration is building a world where criticism of the government carries professional risk.' The warning creates a profound chilling effect on legal advocacy, as defense attorneys must now weigh whether public criticism of prosecutorial overreach could harm their clients' interests—a calculation that fundamentally undermines the adversarial justice system and attorney-client relationships. The timing is particularly significant given the Trump administration's February 2025 executive order pausing FCPA enforcement, subsequent June 2025 revised guidelines weakening corporate accountability, and broader pattern of targeting legal professionals and bar associations that oppose administration policies.

The New York City Bar Association had previously condemned Blanche's November 7, 2025 remarks at the Federalist Society conference where he declared the administration was 'at war' with federal judges and invited young lawyers to 'join the fight' against 'activist judges.' In April 2025, Blanche issued a memorandum banning DOJ employees from participating in American Bar Association events and cutting grants to the ABA after the organization criticized the administration's policies and joined litigation opposing Trump's executive orders. This escalating pattern of intimidation—targeting judges, bar associations, and now individual defense attorneys—represents a systematic assault on the independence of the legal profession and the rule of law. Legal ethics experts expressed alarm that a senior DOJ official would openly acknowledge monitoring lawyers' speech and suggest professional consequences for criticism, actions that violate fundamental principles of attorney independence, client advocacy, and First Amendment protections.
