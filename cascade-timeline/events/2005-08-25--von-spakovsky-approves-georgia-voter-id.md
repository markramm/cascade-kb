title: Hans von Spakovsky Overrules DOJ Career Staff to Approve Georgia Voter ID Law Targeting Black Voters
summary: 'Hans von Spakovsky, serving as Counsel to the Assistant Attorney General for Civil Rights at the U.S. Department of Justice, led the department''s approval of Georgia''s controversial photo ID law in August 2005 despite unanimous objections from career Justice Department attorneys and analysts who determined the law would disproportionately disenfranchise African American voters in violation of the Voting Rights Act. Von Spakovsky''s approval, which overruled the recommendations of career staff who had spent months analyzing the law''s impact, represented a stark example of political appointees politicizing civil rights enforcement to advance voter suppression objectives. A federal judge would later strike down the law as akin to a Jim Crow-era poll tax, vindicating the career staff''s analysis and exposing von Spakovsky''s approval as legally and factually unsupportable.


  Georgia''s 2005 voter ID law (HB 244) required voters to present one of six specific forms of government-issued photo identification at polling places, replacing the previous system that accepted a far broader range of identification including utility bills, bank statements, and other documents establishing identity and residence. The law imposed particular burdens on elderly, low-income, and minority voters who were less likely to possess driver''s licenses or other acceptable photo IDs. Georgia charged $20-$35 for state-issued photo IDs for those without driver''s licenses, creating what critics characterized as a poll tax.


  The Voting Rights Act of 1965 required Georgia and other states with histories of voting discrimination to obtain federal "preclearance" before implementing changes to voting laws. Under Section 5 of the Act, the Justice Department''s Civil Rights Division had to determine whether proposed voting changes would have discriminatory effects on minority voters. Career attorneys and analysts in the Voting Rights Section spent months conducting a thorough analysis of Georgia''s photo ID law, examining data on who possessed the required forms of ID and how the law would affect different demographic groups.


  The career staff''s analysis concluded that the law would disproportionately burden Black voters, who were significantly less likely than white voters to possess the required forms of photo identification. The staff recommended that the Justice Department object to the law''s implementation, preventing it from taking effect. However, political appointees including von Spakovsky overruled this recommendation and approved the law for implementation despite the career staff''s findings of discriminatory impact.


  Adding to the controversy, while Georgia''s voter ID law was under review by the office von Spakovsky supervised, he anonymously published an article endorsing voter ID laws like Georgia''s, creating an obvious conflict of interest and appearance of predetermined conclusions. Von Spakovsky''s covert advocacy for the very type of law he was supposedly objectively evaluating demonstrated that his role in the approval process was driven by political ideology rather than faithful enforcement of the Voting Rights Act.


  The approval proved so controversial that more than half of the career Justice Department staff in the Voting Rights Section left their positions in protest during von Spakovsky''s tenure (2002-2005). Career attorneys who had dedicated their careers to enforcing civil rights laws found themselves repeatedly overruled by political appointees advancing voter suppression policies. The exodus of experienced civil rights attorneys severely damaged the Justice Department''s institutional capacity to enforce voting rights protections, which appears to have been a deliberate objective of von Spakovsky and other political appointees seeking to transform the Civil Rights Division from a voting rights enforcement agency into a voter suppression enabler.


  A federal judge vindicated the career staff''s analysis when Judge Harold Murphy of the U.S. District Court for the Northern District of Georgia struck down the law in October 2005, finding that it constituted an unconstitutional poll tax. Judge Murphy''s ruling noted that the $20-$35 cost of obtaining state-issued photo ID for those without driver''s licenses imposed an unconstitutional financial burden on the right to vote, and that the law would disproportionately prevent poor and minority voters from casting ballots. The ruling confirmed that von Spakovsky''s approval had been both legally and factually wrong.


  Georgia subsequently modified the law to provide free photo IDs, addressing the poll tax concern, and von Spakovsky approved this revised version. However, the modified law still imposed significant non-financial burdens on voters who needed to obtain IDs: traveling to Department of Motor Vehicles offices (often requiring time off work, transportation costs, and childcare arrangements), providing underlying documentation like birth certificates (which themselves cost money and time to obtain), and navigating bureaucratic processes that were particularly challenging for elderly, disabled, and non-English-speaking voters.


  Von Spakovsky''s approval of Georgia''s voter ID law, despite career staff objections and subsequent judicial rejection, became a central issue when President Bush nominated him to the Federal Election Commission in 2006. Senate Democrats opposed the nomination, arguing that von Spakovsky''s oversight of voter laws had been unacceptably partisan. More significantly, career Justice Department staff took the extraordinary step of writing a letter to the Senate opposing von Spakovsky''s nomination—a remarkable public break that demonstrated the depth of concern about his politicization of civil rights enforcement.


  The career staff letter stated that von Spakovsky "played a major role in the implementation of practices which injected partisan political factors into decision-making on enforcement matters and into the hiring process, and included repeated efforts to intimidate career staff." This testimony from career attorneys who had worked under von Spakovsky provided direct evidence that he had systematically politicized the Civil Rights Division to advance voter suppression objectives rather than faithfully enforcing civil rights laws.


  The Georgia voter ID approval was part of a broader pattern during von Spakovsky''s DOJ tenure. Career staff reported that von Spakovsky and other political appointees had blocked investigations of voter discrimination against Native Americans in Minnesota in the weeks before the November 2004 election, had imposed ideological litmus tests in hiring career civil rights attorneys, and had consistently overruled staff recommendations when those recommendations would have protected minority voting rights. The pattern suggested a systematic effort to transform the Civil Rights Division from an agency enforcing voting rights into one enabling voter suppression.


  Von Spakovsky''s DOJ experience would prove valuable when he joined the Heritage Foundation in 2008 to manage the Election Law Reform Initiative. His time overruling career attorneys and approving voter suppression laws despite their discriminatory impacts gave him practical knowledge of how to politicize ostensibly neutral legal processes. At Heritage, he would develop the intellectual justifications and policy frameworks for voter suppression that he had previously implemented through DOJ''s preclearance process.


  The Georgia voter ID case also demonstrated the importance of the Voting Rights Act''s preclearance requirement and the danger of politicizing its enforcement. When career staff faithfully analyzed voting changes for discriminatory impact, they identified laws like Georgia''s that would disenfranchise minority voters. But when political appointees like von Spakovsky overruled career staff based on partisan political objectives, the preclearance process became a rubber stamp for voter suppression. The 2013 Supreme Court decision in Shelby County v. Holder that invalidated the preclearance requirement would eliminate even this compromised oversight, opening the door for massive expansion of voter suppression laws.


  Von Spakovsky''s Georgia voter ID approval in 2005 exemplified how conservative movement infrastructure operated to suppress voting rights: political appointees placed in enforcement positions systematically undermine civil rights protections, conservative think tanks develop intellectual justifications for suppression, ALEC drafts model legislation, state legislatures enact the laws, and conservative media promotes voter fraud narratives to provide political cover. Von Spakovsky participated in multiple stages of this coordination: implementing suppression as a DOJ political appointee, developing intellectual infrastructure at Heritage Foundation, and coordinating with ALEC on model legislation.

In August 2005, Hans von Spakovsky, serving as Counsel to the Assistant Attorney General for Civil Rights at the U.S. Department of Justice, led the department's approval of Georgia's photo voter ID law (HB 244) despite unanimous objections from career Justice Department attorneys. The career staff's months-long analysis concluded the law would disproportionately burden Black voters — who were significantly less likely to possess the six required forms of photo identification — and recommended that DOJ object under the Voting Rights Act's Section 5 preclearance requirement. Von Spakovsky overruled the career staff and approved the law for implementation.

The controversy was compounded by the revelation that, while Georgia's law was under review in his office, von Spakovsky had anonymously published an article endorsing voter ID laws like Georgia's — a clear conflict of interest. The career staff exodus was severe: more than half the attorneys in the Voting Rights Section left their positions in protest during his tenure (2002-2005). A federal judge subsequently struck down the original law in October 2005, ruling it constituted an unconstitutional poll tax because it charged $20-$35 for state-issued photo IDs.

Von Spakovsky's approval of a modified, free-ID version of the law remained controversial. When President Bush nominated him to the Federal Election Commission in 2006, career DOJ attorneys took the extraordinary step of writing a letter to the Senate opposing his confirmation, stating he "played a major role in the implementation of practices which injected partisan political factors into decision-making." The Senate never confirmed him; he served only as a recess appointment. He would later join the Heritage Foundation in 2008 to build the intellectual infrastructure for voter suppression laws nationally.
