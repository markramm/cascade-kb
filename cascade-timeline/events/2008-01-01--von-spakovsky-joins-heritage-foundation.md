summary: 'Hans von Spakovsky joined the Heritage Foundation in 2008 as manager of the Civil Justice Reform Initiative, a position that would soon evolve into his leadership of Heritage''s Election Law Reform Initiative—the organizational platform from which he would become, according to voting rights advocates, "the intellectual godfather of the conservative anti-voting movement." Von Spakovsky''s recruitment to Heritage represented a strategic investment in building the policy and legal infrastructure for systematic voter suppression, coordinating Heritage''s research and advocacy with the American Legislative Exchange Council (ALEC), state-level Republican legislatures, and conservative legal networks.


  Prior to joining Heritage, von Spakovsky had spent 2002-2005 as Counsel to the Assistant Attorney General for Civil Rights at the U.S. Department of Justice, where he became notorious among career attorneys for pushing aggressive interpretations of voting laws designed to restrict minority voting access. He subsequently served two years as a member of the Federal Election Commission (2006-2007), where his confrontational approach and rigid conservative positions on campaign finance and voting issues alienated colleagues across the political spectrum. His FEC nomination had been so controversial that the Senate never confirmed him; he served only as a recess appointment before being forced to step down when it became clear he could not win confirmation.


  Von Spakovsky''s academic credentials included a law degree from Vanderbilt University School of Law (1984) and a bachelor''s degree from MIT (1981). Between his education and government service, he worked for 17 years as a government affairs consultant, in corporate legal departments, and in private practice—experience that familiarized him with the corporate interests that would fund Heritage and ALEC''s voter suppression initiatives.


  At Heritage, von Spakovsky would manage the Election Law Reform Initiative, researching and analyzing campaign finance laws, voter identification requirements, federal enforcement of voting rights laws, election administration procedures, and allegations of election fraud. The Initiative would maintain Heritage''s Election Fraud database, which purported to document "proven cases of election fraud from all over the nation"—a database that voting rights advocates criticized as vastly overstating the prevalence of voter fraud while ignoring the far more significant problem of voter suppression.


  Von Spakovsky''s work at Heritage complemented and coordinated with ALEC''s voter suppression legislative template development. After Barack Obama''s election in November 2008, von Spakovsky would work closely with ALEC to produce model voter ID bills designed to restrict voting access. In 2009, ALEC''s Public Safety and Elections Task Force approved the "Voter ID Act" model legislation, with von Spakovsky''s Heritage Foundation research providing the intellectual justification for these restrictive laws. Von Spakovsky spoke repeatedly at ALEC conferences, providing state legislators with talking points, research citations, and legal arguments they could use to defend voter ID laws against challenges.


  The coordination between Heritage and ALEC exemplified the conservative infrastructure''s multi-layered approach: ALEC drafted model legislation that corporate members and conservative foundations funded; Heritage provided policy research and legal analysis giving the legislation intellectual credibility; state-level conservative think tanks (coordinated through the State Policy Network, which Scaife also funded heavily) promoted the legislation as necessary reform; and conservative media outlets amplified claims of voter fraud to generate public support. Von Spakovsky operated at the center of this coordination, connecting Heritage''s research capacity with ALEC''s legislative drafting and state-level implementation.


  Von Spakovsky''s central argument—that widespread voter fraud required strict photo ID requirements and other voting restrictions—contradicted the overwhelming consensus of election administration experts and academic researchers who found voter impersonation fraud to be exceptionally rare. Numerous investigations, including those by the Bush administration''s own Justice Department, had failed to find evidence of significant voter fraud. Yet von Spakovsky consistently promoted exaggerated fraud claims, cherry-picking isolated incidents and misrepresenting statistical data to justify restrictions that would disproportionately prevent minority, elderly, student, and low-income voters from casting ballots.


  The timing of von Spakovsky''s Heritage appointment proved consequential. Joining in 2008, just as Obama''s election demonstrated the political power of expanded voter participation, von Spakovsky helped Heritage pivot quickly to develop the intellectual infrastructure for a coordinated voter suppression response. When Republicans won control of numerous state legislatures in the 2010 midterm elections, the Heritage-ALEC voter suppression infrastructure was ready to deploy. In 2011, approximately 34 states introduced nearly identical voter ID bills based on the ALEC model that von Spakovsky had helped develop and promote.


  Von Spakovsky''s Heritage position also connected him to the Council for National Policy (CNP), the secretive coordinating organization for conservative movement leaders. Through CNP networks, von Spakovsky could coordinate voter suppression strategies with other movement leaders, ensuring that Heritage''s research, ALEC''s legislation, state-level implementation, legal defense strategies, and media messaging all worked synergistically.


  Beyond voter ID, von Spakovsky''s Heritage work encompassed broader election administration issues designed to reduce voter participation: restrictions on early voting, limitations on absentee voting, aggressive voter roll purges, barriers to voter registration, and opposition to automatic voter registration or same-day registration. In each area, von Spakovsky developed policy arguments and legal justifications that Republican state legislators could cite when implementing restrictive measures.


  Von Spakovsky also became a prominent media spokesperson for voter suppression initiatives, appearing on conservative media outlets to promote fraud claims and defend restrictive voting laws. His Heritage affiliation provided credibility—he could be introduced as a scholar from a respected think tank rather than a partisan operative, even though his scholarship consisted primarily of advocacy for policies that benefited Republican electoral prospects.


  His work extended to training state legislators and conservative activists in voter suppression strategies. Heritage provided resources, talking points, and legal guidance to state legislators implementing ALEC model bills. Von Spakovsky personally testified before state legislatures considering voter ID laws, providing what appeared to be expert testimony but was actually advocacy for predetermined conservative positions.


  The infrastructure von Spakovsky built at Heritage would prove essential to Republican voter suppression efforts over the following decade and beyond. When Trump established the Presidential Advisory Commission on Election Integrity in 2017 (disbanded after failing to find evidence of significant fraud), von Spakovsky served as a key member. When Heritage Action (Heritage''s advocacy arm) launched a $24 million campaign in 2021 to push voter restriction bills in eight key states following Biden''s 2020 victory, it built on the intellectual and organizational foundation von Spakovsky had established.


  Von Spakovsky''s 2008 arrival at Heritage marked the institutionalization of voter suppression as a core conservative policy priority, supported by dedicated organizational capacity, sustained funding, coordination with legislative and legal networks, and integration into the broader conservative movement infrastructure that had been built over the preceding 35 years since the Heritage Foundation''s establishment.

Hans von Spakovsky joined the Heritage Foundation in 2008 as manager of the Civil Justice Reform Initiative, a position that would evolve into leadership of Heritage's Election Law Reform Initiative. Voting rights advocates would later characterize him as "the intellectual godfather of the conservative anti-voting movement." Von Spakovsky brought direct experience from his 2002-2005 tenure at the DOJ Civil Rights Division, where he overruled career attorneys to approve voter suppression laws including Georgia's photo ID legislation, and from his 2006-2007 recess appointment to the Federal Election Commission. His Heritage position institutionalized voter suppression as a central conservative policy priority.

At Heritage, von Spakovsky managed the Election Fraud database — which critics said vastly overstated the prevalence of in-person voter fraud — and produced research and legal arguments justifying restrictive voting laws. His work coordinated closely with the American Legislative Exchange Council (ALEC), which approved a model "Voter ID Act" in 2009 based substantially on von Spakovsky's frameworks. Following Barack Obama's 2008 election, which demonstrated the power of expanded minority voter turnout, Heritage and ALEC rapidly deployed this infrastructure. By 2011, approximately 34 states introduced nearly identical voter ID bills based on the ALEC model.

Von Spakovsky also served as a prominent media spokesperson for voter suppression initiatives, lending Heritage's think-tank credibility to what critics described as partisan advocacy for policies that disproportionately benefited Republican electoral prospects. His infrastructure-building at Heritage proved consequential: when President Trump established the Presidential Advisory Commission on Election Integrity in 2017, von Spakovsky was a key member, and when Heritage Action launched a $24 million voter suppression campaign in 2021 targeting eight states, it built directly on the organizational foundation von Spakovsky had established.
