Homeland Security Secretary Kristi Noem appeared before the Senate Judiciary Committee on March 3, 2026 for her first major oversight hearing since the deaths of two U.S. citizens killed by federal immigration agents in Minneapolis. The nearly four-hour session produced extraordinary bipartisan condemnation, with Republican Sen. Thom Tillis of North Carolina calling her tenure "a disaster" and threatening to grind Senate business to a halt until she answered outstanding accountability questions. Democrats pushed for her resignation. Noem refused to retract characterizations of the killed Americans as terrorists.

## The "Domestic Terrorist" Controversy

The most charged exchanges concerned Noem's public labeling of Renee Macklin Good and Alex Pretti — two U.S. citizens shot and killed by federal agents during Operation Metro Surge in January 2026 — as participants in "domestic terrorism." Video evidence and witness accounts quickly contradicted that characterization after their deaths. At the hearing, Noem offered a semantic hedge: "I did not call him a domestic terrorist. I said it appeared to be an incident of domestic terrorism." She declined to apologize or retract the claim.

Sen. Dick Durbin pressed Noem on whether the characterization had calmed the situation; she defended it rather than walk it back. Sen. Adam Schiff told Noem her statements had "caused endless injury to the victims' families on the basis of God knows what." Even Acting ICE Director Todd Lyons had previously told a Senate Homeland Security Committee hearing in February that he did not believe Good and Pretti were domestic terrorists — directly contradicting his own secretary.

## Republican Rupture: Tillis

The most dramatic moment came from Sen. Thom Tillis, who is not on the Judiciary Committee but whose pointed interventions drew wide attention. Tillis told Noem: "What we've seen is a disaster under your leadership, Ms. Noem. A disaster." He focused on her refusal to cooperate with a DHS Inspector General investigation, citing a letter documenting 10 instances in which investigators were misled. Tillis threatened to block all administration nominees and halt Senate committee proceedings until Noem answered his questions within two weeks.

## The $220 Million Ad Campaign

Republican Sen. John Kennedy of Louisiana questioned Noem about a $220 million taxpayer-funded advertising campaign that featured her prominently — including one spot showing her on horseback with Mount Rushmore in the background. The contracts had gone to Republican consulting firms with ties to Noem and DHS aides. Kennedy suggested the campaign had placed "the president in a terribly awkward spot" by making the secretary the face of the administration's immigration brand in a manner that blended government communications with personal political promotion.

## Democratic Demands

Democratic senators, including Cory Booker, demanded Noem's resignation and accused DHS of violating civil rights and the separation of powers. Booker accused her agency of "terrorizing schoolchildren and breaking into homes." Democrats pressed her on agency spending, arrest tactics that swept in U.S. citizens, and deportation targets with no criminal records.

## Context: DHS Funding Crisis

The hearing occurred amid an ongoing DHS funding shutdown, which Noem called "reckless" and "unnecessary" — while offering few details on its resolution. Her agency had been operating through a bureaucratic standoff that curtailed some functions.

## Significance

The hearing represented the most significant congressional accountability moment for Operation Metro Surge and its aftermath. That both Republican and Democratic senators were willing to directly and publicly criticize Noem — and that the criticism cut across issues of constitutional violations, self-dealing contracts, and the killing of U.S. citizens — illustrated the breadth of the institutional failures under her leadership. Tillis's threat to block nominees was particularly consequential: it signaled that the administration's capture of enforcement machinery had produced political costs even within its own coalition. Noem's refusal to retract the "domestic terrorist" framing, even after it had been contradicted by her own agency's director, confirmed a posture in which accountability to the truth is subordinated to enforcement narrative management.
