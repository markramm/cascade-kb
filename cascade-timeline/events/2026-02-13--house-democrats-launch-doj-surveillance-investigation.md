House Judiciary Ranking Member Jamie Raskin, Immigration Subcommittee Ranking Member Pramila Jayapal, and Oversight Committee Ranking Member Robert Garcia launched a formal investigation into DOJ's "clandestine congressional surveillance operation" after Attorney General Bondi was photographed with Representative Jayapal's detailed search history from the Epstein files reading room. The lawmakers demanded DOJ immediately cease tracking and cataloguing members' searches, calling it an attempt to "obstruct and intimidate Members of Congress."

The investigation revealed DOJ organized the file review process under false pretenses. While the authorization letter disclosed DOJ would log members' "dates and times of access," it made no mention that individual search histories would be tracked, documented, or disseminated. Members were required to travel to a DOJ annex, use DOJ-controlled computers with limited availability, navigate cumbersome software under DOJ staff watch, with congressional staff barred from assisting or even entering the building. More than a dozen Democratic members and at least one Republican (Thomas Massie) reviewed files and may have been subjected to surveillance.

The lawmakers' letter demanded: (1) a full accounting of the surveillance operation including all members whose searches were tracked; (2) explanation of why DOJ collected the information and how it was used in preparation for Bondi's testimony; (3) details on how data was gathered and stored; (4) what additional information beyond searches DOJ is collecting on members. They also demanded a protocol overhaul allowing members to review files at the Capitol with select staff assistance, noting that with only four computers available, reviewing 3 million pages would take over seven years while millions more remain withheld.

The investigation represents a direct challenge to DOJ's weaponization of transparency mechanisms as surveillance tools. By creating the appearance of congressional access while maintaining control over the review environment, DOJ converted a transparency requirement into an intelligence-gathering operation against the legislative branch—using members' constitutional oversight activities to prepare political opposition research for executive branch officials testifying before Congress.
