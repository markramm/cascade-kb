  The legislation''s passage highlighted fundamental tensions in the Trump administration''s second-term national security approach: while the president and his Defense Secretary advocated for maximum operational flexibility and aggressive use of military force, Congress—including many Republicans—insisted on maintaining institutional checks and transparency requirements. The boat strike controversy became a focal point for these broader debates about executive power, military transparency, and Congressional oversight in an era of renewed great power competition and unconventional military operations.

However, Congressional staffers countered that the provision would only affect discretionary travel and that Hegseth could immediately restore full budget authority by complying with transparency requirements that should have been met voluntarily.


  The NDAA''s passage represented a significant institutional assertion of Congressional war powers and oversight authority at a moment when many lawmakers feared the erosion of legislative prerogatives in national security decision-making. By embedding specific constraints tied to the Hegseth boat strike controversy within broader defense authorization legislation, Congress signaled its determination to maintain meaningful oversight even as it provided the massive funding increases the Trump administration requested for military modernization and readiness.

On December 17, 2025, the Senate approved the National Defense Authorization Act for fiscal year 2026 by a bipartisan vote of 77-20, authorizing $901 billion in defense programs. The legislation included an unusual provision specifically targeting Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth: language threatening to withhold 25% of his travel budget unless he provided unedited video footage — along with the authorizing orders — for controversial military strikes on alleged drug vessels in international waters near Venezuela to both the House and Senate Armed Services Committees. The boat strike controversy centered on a September 2, 2025 incident in which Navy Admiral Frank "Mitch" Bradley ordered a "double-tap" strike that killed two survivors of an initial boat attack, raising legal and ethical concerns. Admiral Bradley appeared before both committees in a classified briefing shortly before the Senate vote, eliciting contrasting responses — Republicans largely backed the counternarcotics campaign, while Democrats expressed alarm over the operation's legal basis and the administration's refusal to share comprehensive information with oversight bodies.

Beyond the Hegseth oversight provisions, the NDAA codified significant constraints on Trump administration military policy. The bill required Congressional notification before reducing U.S. troop levels in Europe or NATO countries, mandated Pentagon consultation before removing senior military officials, and maintained a floor of at least 76,000 U.S. troops and equipment in Europe. These requirements reflected deep Congressional concern about potential unilateral strategic shifts — including fears that Trump might withdraw from NATO or dramatically reduce the U.S. military presence supporting Ukraine and European allies — and represented an institutional assertion of legislative war powers authority. The legislation also authorized a 3.8% military pay raise and funded other national security priorities requested by the administration.

The bill's bipartisan 77-20 vote margin reflected the complex political dynamics of the moment: most Republican senators wanted to deliver a large defense authorization while privately sharing some Democratic concerns about operational transparency and legal compliance under Hegseth's leadership. The legislation was passed in the context of ongoing Pentagon Inspector General scrutiny of Hegseth's use of the Signal messaging application to share sensitive military information that allegedly risked servicemember safety. Defense Department officials privately characterized the travel budget withholding provision as micromanagement, while Congressional staffers countered that it would only affect discretionary travel and that Hegseth could immediately restore full authority by complying with transparency requirements that should have been met voluntarily.
