At the National Prayer Breakfast on February 5, 2026, Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth declared "America was founded as a Christian nation. It remains a Christian nation in our DNA, if we can keep it," adding that public officials have "a sacred duty 250 years on to glorify him." Most significantly, Hegseth stated that warriors willing to "lay down his life for his unit, his country, and his Creator" would "find eternal life" — a theological claim that military service for the United States constitutes a path to Christian salvation. Multiple theologians, including evangelical scholars, condemned the remarks as theologically aberrant, drawing comparisons to statements by the Russian Orthodox patriarch about soldiers killed in Ukraine and medieval crusade theology.

The remarks represented a direct continuation of Hegseth's pattern of using his position as the civilian head of the U.S. military — which is constitutionally required to serve personnel of all faiths — to advance Christian nationalist ideology. Military.com reported complaints from service members and contractors who feared career consequences for failing to participate in Hegseth's religious activities, documinating coercive pressure on a religiously diverse force. The conflation of American military service with Christian salvation also carried implications for the treatment of non-Christian service members as potentially less spiritually committed to the mission.

As Secretary of Defense, Hegseth's statements carry institutional weight far beyond personal belief: they signal to commanders and chaplains that Christian nationalist framing is favored, shape the environment in which non-Christian service members operate, and normalize the use of the military as an instrument of religious-nationalist ideology. Combined with his February 18 invitation of theocrat Doug Wilson to lead a Pentagon prayer service, the breakfast remarks form part of a systematic effort to redefine the U.S. Armed Forces as an explicitly Christian institution.
