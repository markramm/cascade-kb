By March 3, 2026, the Military Religious Freedom Foundation (MRFF) had received more than 200 complaints from active-duty military personnel documenting a systemic pattern: commanders across every branch of the U.S. military were framing Operation Epic Fury — the U.S.-Israeli strikes on Iran launched February 28 — in explicitly Christian end-times theological terms, telling troops the war was divinely ordained to trigger Armageddon and the Second Coming of Jesus Christ.

Complaints arrived from more than 40 units stationed across at least 30 military installations throughout the Middle East. One noncommissioned officer reported that their commander "opened up the combat readiness status briefing by urging us to not be 'afraid'" and informed troops "this was 'all part of God's divine plan,'" referencing the Book of Revelation and the imminent return of Jesus Christ. Another NCO documented a commander stating directly: "President Trump has been anointed by Jesus to light the signal fire in Iran to cause Armageddon and mark his return to Earth."

MRFF president Mikey Weinstein described a culture of "unrestricted euphoria" among commanders, with many expressing particular enthusiasm about the anticipated casualties — framing the war's bloodiness as a theological requirement. Complainants documented commanders who were "especially delighted with how graphic this battle will be, zeroing in on how bloody all of this must become in order to fulfill and be in 100% accordance with fundamentalist Christian end of the world eschatology."

MRFF attributed the pattern directly to Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth's systematic campaign to embed evangelical Christianity throughout Pentagon command culture: his monthly Christian prayer services at the Pentagon, his statement at the National Prayer Breakfast that U.S. soldiers who die for their country "find eternal life," his declaration that America "remains a Christian nation in our DNA," and his invitation of self-described Christian nationalist pastor Doug Wilson — who advocates repealing women's suffrage and criminalizing homosexuality — to lead an official Pentagon worship service in February 2026.

CAIR issued a formal condemnation of what it characterized as the Pentagon's use of "dangerous anti-Muslim 'holy war' rhetoric to justify Iran bombing," noting that framing a war against a majority-Muslim nation as a biblically mandated Christian crusade constituted both a First Amendment violation and a strategic liability that inflamed anti-American sentiment across the Muslim world.

The complaints represented the most documented evidence to date of Christian nationalist theology penetrating the operational command culture of the U.S. military — not merely as the personal beliefs of individual service members, but as official ideological framing communicated through the chain of command in pre-combat briefings. Military law experts noted that commanders using their authority to advance specific religious interpretations of a military operation likely violated regulations prohibiting the use of official positions to endorse religion, as well as the First Amendment's Establishment Clause.
