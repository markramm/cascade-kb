title: Leonard Leo Directs Hidden Payments to Ginni Thomas Through Kellyanne Conway, Instructs "No Mention of Ginni"
summary: 'Conservative judicial activist Leonard Leo directed $25,000 in payments to Ginni Thomas, wife of Supreme Court Justice Clarence Thomas, while explicitly instructing that there be "no mention of Ginni" in the paperwork, according to documents obtained by The Washington Post in 2023. In January 2012, Leo told Kellyanne Conway, a GOP pollster who would later serve as a senior aide to President Trump, to "give" Ginni Thomas "another $25K" while emphasizing the need to conceal her involvement. The payments, routed through Conway''s firm and funded by Leo''s Judicial Education Project, totaled $80,000 between June 2011 and June 2012—the same period when Leo''s organization filed its first amicus brief before the Supreme Court in the landmark voting rights case Shelby v. Holder, a case on which Justice Clarence Thomas would rule.


  The payment scheme worked as follows: Leo''s Judicial Education Project (later renamed The 85 Fund) paid Conway''s polling firm, The Polling Company, which in turn paid Ginni Thomas''s Liberty Consulting firm for what was billed as "Constitution Polling and Opinion Consulting." The circuitous payment structure and Leo''s explicit instruction to keep Ginni Thomas''s name off billing paperwork suggested an intent to conceal the financial relationship between Leo, one of the most powerful figures in conservative judicial politics, and the wife of a Supreme Court justice who would rule on cases where Leo''s organizations filed briefs and where Leo-selected judges served.


  Leonard Leo had emerged as the most influential figure in conservative judicial selection, serving as executive vice president of the Federalist Society and later founding a network of dark money organizations that would spend hundreds of millions of dollars to influence judicial appointments and court cases. Leo personally managed judicial selection for both Bush administrations and the Trump administration, effectively controlling conservative Supreme Court appointments. His Judicial Education Project (and its affiliated dark money network) filed amicus briefs, funded litigation, and organized advocacy campaigns in cases before the courts where he had placed judges.


  The timing of the payments proved particularly significant. The Judicial Education Project filed its first-ever Supreme Court amicus brief in 2012—the same year as the payments to Ginni Thomas—in Shelby County v. Holder, the case that would ultimately strike down Section 5 of the Voting Rights Act. Justice Clarence Thomas voted with the 5-4 conservative majority to invalidate the provision that required states with histories of voting discrimination to obtain federal preclearance before changing voting laws. The decision opened the door for the voter suppression efforts that Heritage Foundation, ALEC, and state-level conservative organizations had been developing since 2009.


  Ginni Thomas''s Liberty Consulting firm received $80,000 total between June 2011 and June 2012, according to financial records. Conway''s Polling Company sent billing to the Judicial Education Project for $25,000 labeled "Supplement for Constitution Polling and Opinion Consulting." The nature of the work Ginni Thomas allegedly performed remained vague, with Leo later claiming it involved polling and opinion research unrelated to Supreme Court business. However, the deliberate concealment of her name and the routing of payments through an intermediary suggested the arrangement was designed to avoid transparency rather than reflecting routine consulting work.


  When the payments were revealed by The Washington Post in May 2023, Leo defended the arrangement by stating: "Knowing how disrespectful, malicious and gossipy people can be, I have always tried to protect the privacy of Justice Thomas and Ginni." He insisted that "The work she did here did not involve anything connected with either the court''s business or with other legal issues." However, this explanation failed to address why legitimate consulting work would require explicitly instructing that Ginni Thomas''s name be kept off paperwork, or why the consulting fees needed to be routed through an intermediary rather than paid directly.


  The payments revealed a financial relationship between the conservative judicial network''s most powerful operative and the spouse of a Supreme Court justice, raising profound questions about conflicts of interest and judicial ethics. While Supreme Court justices face minimal ethics requirements compared to lower court judges, the revelation that Leo—who selected conservative judges, funded their confirmation campaigns, financed litigation before the courts, and filed briefs in cases those judges decided—was also making hidden payments to a justice''s spouse suggested a level of coordination and potential corruption that challenged fundamental assumptions about judicial independence.


  The Ginni Thomas payments were part of a broader pattern of financial relationships between the Thomas family and conservative movement funders that would be revealed in subsequent years. In 2023, ProPublica reported that billionaire conservative donor Harlan Crow had provided Justice Clarence Thomas with undisclosed luxury travel, real estate transactions involving Thomas family property, and private school tuition payments for Thomas''s grandnephew—benefits worth potentially millions of dollars that Thomas had failed to disclose on financial disclosure forms. The Leonard Leo payments to Ginni Thomas fit this pattern of hidden financial relationships between the Thomas family and conservative movement figures and funders.


  The payment scheme also illustrated the coordinated nature of conservative judicial politics. Leo controlled both judicial selection (through the Federalist Society) and judicial advocacy (through his network of dark money organizations including the Judicial Education Project, The 85 Fund, and the Concord Fund). He maintained close relationships with conservative Supreme Court justices and their spouses, directed hundreds of millions in dark money to influence court cases and confirmations, and personally managed which cases his organizations would support through amicus briefs and funded litigation. The payments to Ginni Thomas suggested this coordination extended to financial relationships with justices'' family members.


  Kellyanne Conway''s role as intermediary in the payment scheme connected the arrangement to broader conservative movement networks. Conway had worked as a GOP pollster and strategist for years before becoming counselor to President Trump (2017-2020). Her willingness to serve as a conduit for payments to Ginni Thomas while keeping Thomas''s name off paperwork suggested that such arrangements—designed to obscure financial relationships between movement funders and judicial family members—were understood practices within conservative political circles.


  The $80,000 in total payments to Ginni Thomas''s Liberty Consulting between June 2011 and June 2012 represented substantial income, particularly for work whose nature remained deliberately vague. For context, Supreme Court justices'' salaries in 2012 were approximately $213,000 annually, making the $80,000 to Ginni Thomas equivalent to more than one-third of Justice Thomas''s official annual salary. The payments effectively supplemented the Thomas family income with funds from the same conservative networks that brought cases before Justice Thomas and that depended on his votes for victories.


  The revelation of Leo''s payments to Ginni Thomas in 2023 intensified calls for Supreme Court ethics reform and for Justice Thomas''s recusal from cases involving Leo''s organizations or the broader conservative network. Democratic legislators cited the payments as evidence that the Supreme Court''s lack of binding ethics requirements enabled corruption that would be illegal for other federal judges. However, as of late 2023, Justice Thomas had not recused himself from cases involving Leo''s organizations, and the Supreme Court had resisted congressional efforts to impose binding ethics standards on justices.


  The Leo-Thomas payment arrangement exemplified how conservative movement infrastructure operated across multiple domains in coordinated ways: judicial selection, litigation funding, amicus brief filing, and financial relationships with judicial family members all functioned as integrated components of a system designed to capture the federal judiciary. Leonard Leo stood at the center of this coordination, managing the selection of judges, the advocacy before those judges, and apparently the financial supplementation of at least one justice''s family. The system Leo built proved extraordinarily effective—by 2025, six of nine Supreme Court justices were Federalist Society members selected with Leo''s direct involvement, and conservative legal movement priorities (deregulation, voting rights restrictions, abortion bans, gun rights expansion) had prevailed in case after case.

Justice Clarence Thomas subsequently voted with the 5-4 majority that struck down Section 4(b) of the Voting Rights Act, while also writing a concurrence urging the court to go further and invalidate Section 5 entirely. The circuitous payment structure and Leo's explicit instruction to conceal Ginni Thomas's name suggested an intent to obscure the financial relationship between the conservative movement's most powerful judicial operative and a sitting justice's spouse.
