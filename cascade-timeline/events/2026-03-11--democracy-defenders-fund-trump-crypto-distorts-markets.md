On March 11, 2026, the Democracy Defenders Fund published a report documenting how the Trump family's cryptocurrency ventures had been propelled by a combination of undisclosed foreign investment, preferential exchange treatment, and regulatory shielding that together distorted markets and enriched the president. The report's central finding was that USD1 — the stablecoin issued through World Liberty Financial — had rapidly become the third-largest U.S. dollar-denominated stablecoin, a meteoric rise driven not by organic market demand but by a $500 million undisclosed investment from the United Arab Emirates.

The UAE connection ran through Sheikh Tahnoon bin Zayed Al Nahyan, the national security adviser and one of the wealthiest members of Abu Dhabi's ruling family. The investment raised immediate questions about whether a foreign government was using cryptocurrency to gain leverage over a sitting American president. The report noted that the investment was structured in a way that avoided the disclosure requirements that would typically apply to foreign investment in U.S. financial products, and that it coincided with favorable policy decisions affecting the UAE.

Binance, the world's largest cryptocurrency exchange, provided preferential treatment to USD1 that included promotional placement and annual returns of approximately 20 percent — far above market rates and unavailable to competing stablecoins. The report argued this preferential treatment was not coincidental but reflected Binance's own regulatory calculus, as the exchange faced ongoing legal and compliance issues in the United States that gave it strong incentive to cultivate goodwill with the Trump administration. The effect was to create an artificial competitive advantage for a presidential financial product.

The cumulative financial impact was enormous. Trump's net worth had jumped from approximately $4.3 billion to $7.3 billion over eight months, with much of the increase driven by his cryptocurrency holdings. Lee Reiners, a financial regulation expert consulted for the report, noted that the arrangement represented a new category of corruption: a president whose personal wealth was directly tied to the regulatory decisions his administration made about cryptocurrency markets. The report concluded that the combination of foreign investment, exchange favoritism, and regulatory capture had created a self-reinforcing cycle that enriched the president while distorting the broader stablecoin market.
