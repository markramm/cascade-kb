The American Alliance for Equal Rights (AAER) filed a federal civil rights lawsuit on December 3, 2025, in the U.S. District Court for the District of Columbia against the Hispanic Scholarship Fund (HSF), challenging the organization's flagship "HSF Scholars Program" on grounds that limiting eligibility to students "who identify as being of Hispanic Heritage" violates the Civil Rights Act of 1866 by excluding Asian, white, Black, and other non-Hispanic applicants from consideration. The organization, founded and led by Edward Blum—the conservative legal strategist who orchestrated the successful 2023 Supreme Court challenge to affirmative action in college admissions through Students for Fair Admissions—represents the latest front in a coordinated nationwide campaign to dismantle race-conscious programs and diversity, equity, and inclusion (DEI) initiatives through aggressive litigation that weaponizes civil rights laws originally designed to protect minorities against discrimination. The lawsuit seeks to bar HSF from considering ethnicity in any way in its scholarship program, threatening the organization's 50-year mission of addressing educational inequities facing Hispanic students and establishing legal precedent that could devastate private scholarship programs, fellowships, and support structures designed to address historic and ongoing barriers to educational access for underrepresented communities.

The American Alliance for Equal Rights filed its complaint seeking injunctive and declaratory relief to prohibit HSF from "knowing or considering ethnicity in any way" in its HSF Scholars Program, which annually distributes scholarships ranging from $500 to $5,000 to approximately 10,000 students. According to HSF, the organization has distributed over $756 million in scholarships during its 50-year history, providing critical financial support that has enabled hundreds of thousands of Hispanic students to access and complete college education despite significant economic and systemic barriers that disproportionately affect Hispanic families.

Edward Blum founded both the American Alliance for Equal Rights and Students for Fair Admissions, the nonprofit organization that served as the plaintiff in the landmark 2023 Supreme Court case that effectively ended race-conscious affirmative action in college admissions. The 6-3 Supreme Court decision in Students for Fair Admissions v. Harvard marked a devastating blow to educational equity and opened the door for aggressive legal challenges to any programs that consider race or ethnicity in providing opportunities or support to underrepresented students. Blum has built a career and organizational infrastructure around challenging civil rights protections, voting rights safeguards, and diversity initiatives, consistently arguing that race-conscious programs designed to remedy historic discrimination actually constitute illegal discrimination against white and Asian students.

Bloomberg Law reported that Blum estimated the American Alliance for Equal Rights has filed approximately 20 lawsuits since the 2023 Students for Fair Admissions Supreme Court victory, systematically targeting what he describes as "race-exclusive scholarship funds, fellowships, internships" at educational institutions, corporations, and nonprofit organizations across the country. This litigation campaign represents a coordinated assault on programs designed to address educational and economic inequities, weaponizing legal resources and conservative judicial appointments to dismantle structures that have provided pathways to opportunity for students from communities that have faced historic and ongoing discrimination and exclusion.

The complaint employs a Reconstruction-era statute—the Civil Rights Act of 1866—which was enacted to protect formerly enslaved people from discrimination and which prohibits racial discrimination in contracts. Blum's legal strategy inverts the purpose of this civil rights protection, using a law designed to combat discrimination against Black Americans to instead attack programs that seek to remedy ongoing disparities and barriers facing Hispanic students. The lawsuit argues that HSF's Scholars Program constitutes a "contract" because applicants "agree to HSF's terms, assent to its privacy policy, draft several essays, and sign a binding pledge," and therefore the program's limitation to Hispanic students violates the 1866 Act's prohibition on racial discrimination in contracts.

This legal theory, if successful, would have devastating implications far beyond the Hispanic Scholarship Fund. Thousands of private scholarships, fellowships, internships, and support programs sponsored by ethnic organizations, professional associations, religious groups, and community foundations limit eligibility based on group membership, heritage, or identity in order to address specific barriers or support particular communities. If courts accept Blum's argument that such eligibility criteria constitute illegal racial discrimination in contracts, virtually all identity-specific support programs would be vulnerable to legal challenge, eliminating critical resources that help level playing fields distorted by historic and ongoing discrimination.

The American Alliance identifies two unnamed plaintiff members who claim injury from HSF's eligibility criteria: one individual identified as Asian American and one identified as white. The complaint contends these individuals are "ready and able to apply for HSF's program but cannot because of their ethnicity." This framing ignores the reality that Asian Americans and white students have access to thousands of scholarship programs without Hispanic heritage requirements, while Hispanic students—who face significant educational equity gaps including lower college enrollment rates, higher dropout rates, and greater financial barriers than white students—have far fewer scholarship resources specifically designed to address the particular challenges their communities face.

Inside Higher Ed reported that the lawsuit specifically challenges the HSF Scholars Program, the organization's flagship initiative that provides not only financial support but also mentorship, career development resources, and community connections that help Hispanic students navigate higher education systems where they are often underrepresented and may lack family members with college experience to provide guidance. The program's holistic approach recognizes that supporting Hispanic student success requires more than money—it requires culturally responsive mentorship, community building, and recognition of the specific challenges first-generation college students and students from immigrant families face in accessing and completing higher education.

The Washington Free Beacon, a conservative publication aligned with Blum's anti-DEI agenda, published an editorial titled "A Talented Student, But the Wrong Ancestry: Why We Filed Suit Against the Hispanic Scholarship Fund," framing the lawsuit as protecting capable students who are excluded based on ethnicity. This framing inverts reality—Hispanic students are systematically disadvantaged in educational access and outcomes compared to white students, facing greater poverty rates, attending under-resourced schools, encountering language barriers for immigrant families, and navigating college application and financial aid systems designed primarily for white middle-class families. Programs like the HSF Scholars Program exist precisely because "talented students" from Hispanic communities face barriers that have nothing to do with their abilities and everything to do with systemic inequities that race-neutral programs fail to address.

The lawsuit's timing and strategy reveal coordination with the broader Trump administration and conservative movement campaign to eliminate DEI initiatives across education, government, and corporate sectors. Throughout 2025, the Trump administration has attacked DEI offices at federal agencies, threatened federal funding for universities that maintain race-conscious programs, and signaled support for litigation challenging private sector diversity initiatives. Blum's lawsuit against HSF fits perfectly within this broader assault, using the courts to accomplish what executive action cannot directly achieve—eliminating private scholarship programs that address racial and ethnic educational inequities.

The complaint's argument that HSF "bans Blacks, Asians, and Native Americans that aren't Hispanic" while potentially "welcoming eligible white applicants" attempts to portray the program as excluding minorities while potentially benefiting some white students, a messaging strategy designed to complicate narratives about who benefits from race-conscious programs. This framing deliberately obscures that Hispanic is an ethnic rather than racial category, that many Hispanic individuals also identify with Black, Indigenous, or other racial categories, and that the fundamental purpose of Hispanic-focused scholarships is addressing educational inequities facing a specific ethnic community regardless of the racial diversity within that community.

The lawsuit threatens the Hispanic Scholarship Fund's ability to fulfill its mission of addressing educational inequities that demonstrably affect Hispanic students. According to Department of Education data, Hispanic students have lower college enrollment rates than white students, higher dropout rates, greater reliance on student loans, and face significant financial barriers that scholarships like those HSF provides help overcome. Hispanic families have lower median wealth than white families, meaning students have less family financial support for college expenses. First-generation college students—disproportionately Hispanic—navigate unfamiliar application, financial aid, and enrollment systems without the family guidance many white students take for granted. These systemic inequities justify targeted scholarship programs that provide both financial support and culturally responsive mentorship to Hispanic students.

If AAER succeeds in forcing HSF to eliminate Hispanic heritage eligibility criteria, the organization would face an impossible choice: abandon its mission of supporting Hispanic students specifically and become a generic scholarship program indistinguishable from thousands of others, or cease operations entirely because its funding sources and donor base specifically committed resources to addressing Hispanic educational inequities. This forced choice exemplifies how Blum's litigation campaign seeks not to promote genuine equality of opportunity but to eliminate programs that acknowledge and address ongoing racial and ethnic inequities.

The legal strategy of using the Civil Rights Act of 1866 represents sophisticated manipulation of civil rights laws for anti-civil rights purposes. The 1866 Act was enacted to protect freedmen from discrimination in the aftermath of slavery, providing federal protection for contract rights, property rights, and equal treatment under law when Southern states were enacting Black Codes designed to perpetuate racial subordination despite the formal abolition of slavery. Using this statute—passed explicitly to protect Black Americans from racial discrimination—to attack programs that address ongoing educational inequities facing Hispanic students represents a profound perversion of civil rights law, inverting protections designed for subordinated groups into weapons against programs that seek to remedy inequality.

The lawsuit also ignores the extensive Supreme Court precedent recognizing that private organizations, including scholarship funds, have associational freedom to define their membership and beneficiaries based on group identity and mission. Organizations like the Knights of Columbus, Jewish Community Centers, historically Black fraternities and sororities, and countless other membership-based associations limit participation or benefits based on religious, ethnic, or other identity criteria without violating civil rights laws because these criteria advance the organizations' legitimate purposes of serving particular communities and preserving cultural traditions and mutual support networks. Treating scholarship eligibility criteria as illegal discrimination threatens these organizational freedoms and the pluralistic civil society they support.

Conservative judicial appointments made during the Trump administration's first term, and expanded during his second term, create favorable conditions for Blum's litigation strategy. Federal courts increasingly stocked with judges hostile to affirmative action and skeptical of race-conscious remedies for discrimination provide sympathetic forums for lawsuits challenging DEI initiatives and programs that acknowledge racial and ethnic identity. The Supreme Court's 2023 affirmative action decision signaled that the conservative supermajority would aggressively limit consideration of race in educational contexts, and lower courts dominated by Trump appointees have taken this signal as license to strike down diversity programs and race-conscious initiatives across multiple domains.

The American Alliance for Equal Rights operates as a well-funded litigation machine backed by conservative donors who view attacking DEI and diversity initiatives as ideologically essential and politically advantageous. Blum has cultivated relationships with conservative legal organizations, think tanks, and donor networks that provide resources for sustained litigation campaigns targeting civil rights protections, voting rights, and diversity programs. The organization's capacity to file approximately 20 lawsuits in less than two years since the 2023 Supreme Court decision demonstrates significant financial backing and organizational infrastructure dedicated to dismantling programs that address racial and ethnic inequities.

The case also reflects broader conservative hostility to recognizing ongoing effects of historic discrimination and contemporary barriers facing communities of color. Conservative legal ideology increasingly embraces colorblindness as an absolute principle, arguing that any acknowledgment of race or ethnicity in allocating opportunities or resources constitutes illegal discrimination regardless of whether such acknowledgment is designed to remedy past discrimination or address ongoing inequities. This colorblind absolutism conveniently ignores that race-neutral policies and programs systematically reproduce advantages for white Americans and disadvantages for communities of color because they operate in societies still structured by centuries of racial discrimination and ongoing patterns of racial inequality in wealth, educational opportunity, neighborhood resources, and institutional access.

Hispanic educational organizations and civil rights groups have condemned the lawsuit as an attack on programs that address real and persistent inequities. The Hispanic Scholarship Fund noted that its programs exist because Hispanic students continue to face significant barriers to educational access and completion, barriers that race-neutral scholarship programs fail to address because they don't account for the specific challenges facing Hispanic students including language, immigration status, first-generation college attendance, and economic disadvantages disproportionately affecting Hispanic families. Eliminating Hispanic-specific scholarships would remove critical resources that have enabled hundreds of thousands of students to access higher education and economic mobility that would otherwise remain out of reach.

Educational researchers and policy experts have documented that targeted scholarship programs for underrepresented students are essential for addressing persistent educational inequities and diversifying higher education institutions and professional fields where Hispanic individuals remain underrepresented. Programs like HSF Scholars don't exclude non-Hispanic students from higher education opportunities—they provide additional support to Hispanic students who face systematic barriers, helping level a playing field tilted by historic and ongoing discrimination. Characterizing such programs as illegal discrimination inverts reality, treating efforts to remedy inequality as if they created inequality, and ignoring the vast infrastructure of advantage that white students access through family wealth, social networks, well-funded suburban schools, legacy admissions, and countless other mechanisms that systematically benefit white students without requiring explicit racial criteria.

As the lawsuit proceeds through federal district court and likely eventual appeals regardless of initial outcome, the Hispanic Scholarship Fund faces significant legal expenses defending its programs and potential disruption of scholarship operations while litigation is pending. Even if HSF ultimately prevails, the litigation creates chilling effects for other organizations considering establishing or maintaining identity-specific scholarship programs, as the threat of expensive legal challenges and uncertainty about legal standards deters programs that might otherwise address educational inequities. This chilling effect serves Blum's broader strategic goals even when particular lawsuits fail, as organizations may eliminate or modify programs to avoid litigation rather than defend their legality and mission.

The attack on the Hispanic Scholarship Fund represents one battle in a broader war against diversity, equity, and inclusion initiatives across American society. The same legal theories and litigation strategies being deployed against HSF are simultaneously being used to challenge corporate diversity programs, law firm fellowships for minority students, medical school initiatives to increase representation of Black and Hispanic physicians, and countless other efforts to address persistent underrepresentation and inequity. The coordinated nature of this campaign—approximately 20 lawsuits in under two years by one organization alone, plus additional challenges by allied conservative legal groups—reveals that this is not organic opposition to particular programs but a well-resourced, strategically planned assault on the entire infrastructure of programs and initiatives designed to acknowledge and address racial and ethnic inequity.

The ultimate stakes extend far beyond scholarship dollars. If Blum's litigation campaign succeeds in eliminating identity-conscious programs and establishing legal precedent that any consideration of race or ethnicity constitutes illegal discrimination, American society will lack tools to address persistent and well-documented inequities in education, employment, healthcare, housing, and countless other domains where centuries of discrimination and ongoing structural racism continue to produce dramatically different outcomes for communities of color compared to white Americans. The conservative movement's vision of mandatory colorblindness would prohibit acknowledging these inequities or implementing targeted programs to address them, effectively locking in current patterns of inequality by forbidding efforts to remedy them while claiming to promote equality by treating unequal situations identically.

The lawsuit against the Hispanic Scholarship Fund exemplifies how authoritarian movements weaponize law and courts to attack civil society institutions that promote equity and serve communities marginalized by dominant power structures. Rather than championing equal opportunity, Blum's litigation campaign eliminates programs that help level playing fields distorted by historic and ongoing discrimination. Rather than protecting civil rights, the American Alliance for Equal Rights inverts civil rights laws to attack programs that address the very inequities these laws were meant to remedy. And rather than promoting fairness in education, the assault on Hispanic scholarships removes resources that help talented students overcome systematic barriers to educational access and success, ensuring that educational opportunities remain concentrated among those who already benefit from historic and ongoing advantages rather than being shared more equitably across communities that have faced discrimination and exclusion.
