President Clinton signed the National Voter Registration Act (NVRA), known as "Motor Voter," requiring states to offer voter registration when citizens apply for driver's licenses, at public assistance offices, and through mail-in registration. The law aimed to reverse decades of declining voter registration by removing bureaucratic barriers, but faced immediate Republican resistance and uneven implementation.

Motor Voter represented the first major federal voter registration reform since the Voting Rights Act. The legislation required all states (except those with Election Day registration or no voter registration requirements) to provide registration opportunities at DMV offices, public assistance agencies including welfare offices, and military recruitment offices. States were also required to accept a standardized national mail-in registration form.

Congressional Republicans overwhelmingly opposed the bill, with Senate Minority Leader Bob Dole calling it an "open invitation to fraud" and warning it would register "welfare recipients and food stamp users" who would vote Democratic. The partisan divide was stark: the House passed Motor Voter 259-160 with only 20 Republican votes; the Senate required cloture to overcome a filibuster. Republican opposition reflected awareness that expanding registration access would likely benefit Democrats.

Several Republican governors resisted implementation. California's Pete Wilson and Illinois' Jim Edgar refused to comply, requiring Justice Department lawsuits. Other states implemented the law grudgingly with minimal publicity. Public assistance agencies, staffed by state employees in states hostile to the law, often failed to offer registration as required. The "agency registration" provisions proved particularly difficult to enforce.

Motor Voter succeeded in expanding registration access but fell short of transformative impact. Voter registration rates increased modestly, but turnout remained stagnant. Critics noted that making registration easier without addressing other barriers—work schedules, polling place access, voter ID requirements—had limited effect. The law's list maintenance provisions, requiring states to regularly update voter rolls, would later be weaponized for aggressive purge programs targeting minority voters.

The Republican opposition to Motor Voter foreshadowed the party's 21st-century voter suppression strategy. Claims of fraud prevention—without evidence of actual fraud—became the standard justification for restricting access. The same politicians who opposed making registration easier would later champion voter ID laws, reduced early voting, and aggressive purges, demonstrating consistent opposition to expanding electoral participation.
