President Clinton signs the FDA Modernization Act (FDAMA), codifying accelerated drug approval pathways developed during the AIDS crisis while expanding provisions favorable to pharmaceutical manufacturers including streamlined advertising approval. The law accelerates the transformation of FDA from a public health gatekeeper into an industry partner focused on drug approval speed over safety evaluation.

The pharmaceutical industry lobbies intensively for FDAMA, building on the 1992 Prescription Drug User Fee Act (PDUFA) that established industry funding of FDA drug review operations. By making FDA financially dependent on pharmaceutical user fees, Congress creates structural pressure for faster approvals. FDAMA institutionalizes expedited review processes including Fast Track designation and accelerated approval based on surrogate endpoints rather than clinical outcomes.

The law permits pharmaceutical companies to disseminate information about off-label drug uses under certain conditions, expanding marketing opportunities for products beyond their approved indications. While framed as ensuring physicians have access to scientific information, the provision opens pathways for promotional activities that regulators struggle to distinguish from legitimate education.

FDAMA also facilitates the explosion of direct-to-consumer pharmaceutical advertising by directing FDA to develop guidance permitting broadcast advertisements with abbreviated risk disclosures. The United States becomes one of only two countries permitting direct-to-consumer drug advertising, generating billions in pharmaceutical marketing expenditure that drives demand for expensive brand-name medications over generics or non-pharmaceutical interventions.

The user fee model established by PDUFA and expanded by FDAMA fundamentally alters FDA incentives. With review timelines tied to fee agreements and industry representatives participating in policy discussions, FDA becomes increasingly responsive to pharmaceutical company priorities. Drug withdrawals for safety reasons in subsequent years—including Vioxx, causing an estimated 28,000 heart attacks—reveal the consequences of prioritizing approval speed over rigorous safety evaluation. The law establishes the template for regulatory capture through user fee dependency that spreads to other FDA programs and federal agencies.
