The Clinton administration launches Operation Gatekeeper, deploying additional Border Patrol agents, surveillance equipment, and barriers along the San Diego sector of the U.S.-Mexico border. Immigration and Naturalization Service Commissioner Doris Meissner implements the "prevention through deterrence" strategy, which concentrates enforcement resources at traditional urban crossing points to force unauthorized border crossers into remote desert and mountain terrain where the elements serve as "natural barriers." The policy accepts that migrants will continue attempting crossings but anticipates that the increased danger and difficulty will eventually deter them.

Operation Gatekeeper is part of a broader enforcement escalation including Operation Hold-the-Line in El Paso (1993), Operation Safeguard in Tucson (1994), and Operation Rio Grande in Texas (1997). Clinton administration officials explicitly acknowledge the deterrence logic: by making the journey more dangerous, they expect migration to decline. Instead, migrants adapt by hiring smugglers and crossing through increasingly hazardous terrain. Border crossing deaths increase dramatically—from approximately 200 annual deaths before Gatekeeper to over 400 by 2000, with some years exceeding 500 confirmed deaths. Thousands of remains are recovered in the Arizona desert over subsequent decades; many more are never found.

The policy also fails at its stated goal of reducing unauthorized immigration. Apprehension numbers continue increasing through the late 1990s, while the unauthorized immigrant population grows from an estimated 3.5 million in 1990 to over 8 million by 2000. Border enforcement spending increases from $363 million in 1993 to $2.1 billion by 2002, representing what critics call the "enforcement first" consensus that demands ever-larger enforcement budgets while producing neither reduced migration nor reduced deaths. Operation Gatekeeper establishes the bipartisan template for border militarization that persists for decades: Democratic administrations increase enforcement to neutralize Republican attacks while Republicans demand still more. Defense contractors and surveillance technology firms benefit from expanding budgets, creating constituencies invested in perpetual enforcement escalation regardless of outcomes. The policy's predictable death toll and documented failure to reduce migration raise questions about whether deterrence is the actual goal, or whether enforcement theater serves political rather than practical purposes.
