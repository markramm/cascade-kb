In 1997, Michigan State University gymnastics coach Kathie Klages was reportedly aware of the first known allegations against team doctor Larry Nassar when MSU gymnasts told her that Nassar was molesting them during treatment sessions. According to court documents, Klages dismissed the complaints and pressured gymnasts to stay silent about the abuse. This marked the beginning of a pattern of institutional cover-up that would enable Nassar to sexually abuse hundreds of young athletes over the next two decades without accountability.

The 1997 complaints represented the first documented institutional failure in what would become the largest sexual abuse scandal in sports history. Nassar had begun sexually abusing gymnasts in the early 1990s, with one victim reporting she was 8 years old in 1988 when Nassar arrived at the Great Lakes Gymnastics Club in Lansing, Michigan. A civil lawsuit filed in 2018 alleged that in the early 1990s, Nassar had drugged and raped a woman during treatment and videotaped the assault, and that local police and MSU's athletic director became aware of Nassar's conduct but took no action.

The institutional response to the 1997 complaints established a pattern of prioritizing institutional reputation and legal liability over athlete safety that would persist for nearly 20 years. Klages' dismissal of the complaints sent a clear message to victims that reporting abuse would be futile and potentially harmful to their athletic careers. This created an environment of silence and fear that allowed Nassar to continue his predatory behavior while serving as team doctor for the United States women's national gymnastics team from 1996 to 2014.

The failure to investigate the 1997 complaints demonstrated how institutional structures at Michigan State University systematically protected abusers while exposing vulnerable young athletes to continued sexual violence. University officials would later claim ignorance of Nassar's abuse despite receiving reports as early as 1997, but the pattern of dismissing complaints and pressuring victims to stay silent revealed conscious institutional choices to avoid accountability at the expense of athlete welfare. The 1997 complaints marked the beginning of institutional betrayal that would ultimately result in more than 500 victims and settlements totaling nearly $900 million.
