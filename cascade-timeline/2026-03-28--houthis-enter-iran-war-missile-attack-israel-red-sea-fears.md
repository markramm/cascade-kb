On March 28, 2026 — one month into the Iran war — Yemen's Houthi movement launched a barrage of ballistic missiles toward what it described as "sensitive Israeli military sites" in southern Israel, marking the Houthis' formal entry into the conflict. Houthi military spokesperson Yahya Saree announced the attack on Al-Masirah television. The Israeli military reported intercepting the projectiles. The strike was the Houthis' first action since the Iran war began on February 28.

The missile attack on Israel immediately raised alarm about a second front of maritime disruption. With the Strait of Hormuz already effectively closed by Iran, analysts warned that Houthi attacks on commercial shipping in the Red Sea would create a catastrophic double disruption of global trade routes. The Houthis had previously conducted an extensive campaign against Red Sea shipping during the Israel-Hamas war, forcing major shipping lines to reroute around the Cape of Good Hope and significantly reducing traffic through the Suez Canal. Approximately $1 trillion worth of goods passed through the Red Sea annually before the disruptions began.

The potential resumption of Red Sea attacks compounded an already severe global shipping crisis. The Hormuz closure had removed approximately 20 percent of seaborne oil from global markets; a simultaneous Red Sea disruption would force vessels on the alternative Cape of Good Hope route to add weeks and significant costs to voyages between Asia and Europe. Combined, the two chokepoint crises threatened to fundamentally disrupt the maritime trade routes that underpin the global economy.

The Houthis' entry into the war represented yet another front in the cascading regional conflict that critics had warned would follow strikes on Iran. What the administration had framed as a targeted operation against Iran's nuclear program had, within 30 days, expanded to encompass Iran, the Gulf states, Lebanon, and now Yemen — a multi-front conflagration spanning from the Mediterranean to the Arabian Sea with no clear path to resolution.
