On June 11, 1997, Ralph Reed formally resigns as executive director of the Christian Coalition — the organization he had built from $200,000 and 2,000 members in 1989 to $27 million and 1.9 million members at its 1996 peak. He immediately founds Century Strategies, a public relations and political consulting firm based in Atlanta that specializes in Republican electoral campaigns and conservative issue advocacy.

Reed's departure marks a structural transition in the Christian right's history. The Coalition had been the organizational vessel for evangelical political energy — a membership organization, a grassroots network, a voter-guide machine. Century Strategies is something different: a professional political consultancy that sells evangelical voter-mobilization expertise to Republican clients. The shift is from movement organization to political industry. The Christian right's organizing capacity — built through years of volunteer church networks — becomes a monetizable professional service.

**The organizational consequences are immediate and severe.** Within months of Reed's departure, a half-dozen top Coalition officials leave or are forced out. The organization loses members and revenue: from $26 million in 1996 to $17 million in 1997. The FEC files suit alleging illegal coordination with Republican candidates (specifically, a prohibited corporate contribution to Oliver North's 1994 Virginia Senate campaign through mailing list sharing). The IRS rejects the Coalition's tax-exempt status application after years of negotiations, finding its political activities too partisan for 501(c)(3) classification. By 1999, the Coalition restructures into two separate entities — a political committee and a voter-guide nonprofit — as a workaround. By 2001, the organization that had been the dominant force in American evangelical politics is a shadow of its 1994 self.

**The structural meaning for CN lineage:** The Christian Coalition's post-Reed decline is not the end of evangelical electoral infrastructure — it is its disaggregation. The infrastructure Reed built disperses into: (1) the Family Research Council under Gary Bauer, which takes on the D.C. lobbying and policy role; (2) Focus on the Family under Dobson, which takes on the mass-radio mobilization role; (3) Century Strategies and similar consulting firms, which take on the electoral-technical role; and (4) the Council for National Policy, which continues providing elite coordination. The movement decentralizes but does not demobilize. When Reed reconstitutes his electoral operation as the Faith and Freedom Coalition in 2009, he is rebuilding a more distributed version of the same infrastructure for a new Republican coalition that includes the Tea Party and eventually Trump.
