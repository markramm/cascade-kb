James Dobson delivers an ultimatum to the Republican Party at a Council for National Policy (CNP) meeting, followed on March 24, 1998 by a letter to his "Congressional Friends" — an indictment of the Republican Congress for moral failure. Dobson excoriates House Majority Whip Tom DeLay as "argumentative, defensive and accusatory" when confronted with the social conservative movement's frustrations, and warns that he will lead evangelical Christians out of the Republican Party if GOP leaders continue to treat them as a vote-delivery vehicle while delivering nothing on social issues.

The context: Republicans had controlled both chambers since the 1994 revolution, yet had produced no significant pro-life legislation, no school prayer amendment, and no meaningful restriction on pornography. The Clinton-Lewinsky scandal had dominated political oxygen while the social conservative agenda stalled. Dobson's letter is a precise articulation of the structural complaint that has defined the evangelical-GOP alliance ever since: the Republican Party needs evangelical turnout to win elections but does not need to govern according to evangelical priorities once in office.

**The threat is credible.** Dobson's daily radio program reaches an estimated 200 million listeners in 164 countries. Focus on the Family's annual budget exceeds $100 million. His mailing list reaches millions of evangelical households. His voter-guide operation — coordinated with the Family Research Council — can shift turnout in competitive districts. When Dobson says he will walk, Republican strategists run the electoral math: without evangelical turnout at 1994-1996 levels, the GOP cannot maintain its congressional majority.

**What the ultimatum produces:** Rather than calling Dobson's bluff, Republican leadership accommodates. The Federal Marriage Amendment push (2003-2004), the 11-state anti-gay-marriage ballot initiative strategy (2004), and the explicit "values voter" reelection framing of the Bush campaign all follow from this structural dynamic — the Republican Party learns that social conservative demands must be fed constantly or the base will defect. Dobson's 1998 ultimatum is thus load-bearing for everything that follows in the 2000s: it demonstrates that threatening to leave is more effective than actually leaving, and it teaches Republican strategists that the evangelical base must be given symbolic victories on culture-war issues even when substantive policy delivery is absent.

The episode also reveals the asymmetric nature of the alliance: evangelicals provide votes the GOP cannot generate without them; the GOP provides legislative power evangelicals cannot achieve without it. The mutual dependency is the coalition's structural stability and its structural vulnerability. Dobson's 1998 letter is the clearest articulation of that dynamic in the entire middle-period record.
