On August 12, 2013, Attorney General Eric Holder delivers a speech to the American Bar Association announcing the "Smart on Crime" initiative — the first significant federal policy retreat from the mandatory minimum sentencing regime that has driven mass incarceration for three decades. Holder directs federal prosecutors to stop charging low-level, nonviolent drug offenders with offenses that trigger mandatory minimum sentences, instructing them to omit specific drug quantities from charging documents when the defendant meets certain criteria: no ties to large-scale organizations, no significant criminal history, no use of violence.

The directive is significant not for what it changes but for what it acknowledges. For the first time, a sitting attorney general states publicly that the federal criminal justice system is broken by design: "Too many Americans go to too many prisons for far too long, and for no truly good law enforcement reason." Holder cites the statistics directly — the United States accounts for 5% of the world's population and 25% of its prisoners; the federal prison population has grown by almost 800% since 1980; the federal prison system is 40% over capacity; federal spending on incarceration exceeds $80 billion annually.

Smart on Crime also expands compassionate release for elderly and infirm prisoners, directs the U.S. Attorneys' offices to develop community-based alternatives to incarceration, and establishes criteria for declining to prosecute certain marijuana-related offenses in states that have legalized cannabis. Holder frames the initiative in language calculated to appeal across partisan lines — "smart on crime" rather than "soft on crime" — emphasizing fiscal conservatism alongside justice.

The initiative's limitations reveal how deeply mass incarceration is embedded in institutional incentives. The directive is a policy memorandum, not a law — it can be (and is) reversed by the next administration. Jeff Sessions rescinds Holder's charging guidance on his first full day as attorney general in May 2017, directing prosecutors to "charge and pursue the most serious, readily provable offense." The structural architecture of mandatory minimums remains intact: the Rockefeller-model laws, the 1986 and 1988 Anti-Drug Abuse Acts, the ALEC-drafted state legislation, and the 1994 Crime Bill's truth-in-sentencing requirements all continue to operate.

The Smart on Crime initiative also fails to address the private prison industry's structural incentives. While the federal prison population begins a modest decline (from 219,000 in 2013 to 185,000 by 2016), private prison companies respond by shifting to immigration detention, where the same mandatory detention policies guarantee a growing population unaffected by sentencing reform.

Holder's initiative nonetheless marks an inflection point in the politics of incarceration. For the first time since the Rockefeller laws (1973), a senior federal official frames mass incarceration as a policy failure rather than a policy success. The initiative creates political space for bipartisan sentencing reform — the First Step Act (2018) passes with support from both parties — and begins the slow process of establishing that the 40-year experiment in mass incarceration produced neither public safety nor justice, but a system that extracts from the poorest communities and enriches the institutions that cage them.
