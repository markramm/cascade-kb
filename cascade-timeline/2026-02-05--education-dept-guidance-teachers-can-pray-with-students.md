On February 5, 2026, the Trump Department of Education under Secretary Linda McMahon issued new guidance on "Constitutionally Protected Prayer and Religious Expression in Public Elementary and Secondary Schools," superseding the Biden administration's 2023 guidance on the same topic. The most consequential change allowed public school teachers and other school employees to take part in visible prayer with students during the school day — including activities like saying grace together before lunch — so long as it was framed as the teacher's individual expression rather than school-sponsored prayer. McMahon stated: "The Trump Administration is proud to stand with students, parents, and faculty who wish to exercise their First Amendment rights in schools across our great nation."

The guidance stretched the legal framework established by Supreme Court decisions on school prayer in ways that legal scholars said blurred the line between individual religious expression and state establishment. Critics argued that when a teacher prays visibly with students, the inherent power differential makes "voluntary" participation effectively coercive for students from minority faiths or no faith — a concern the guidance acknowledged only superficially by restating that schools may not "coerce or pressure" students. Constitutional law experts noted the guidance appeared designed to encourage the very forms of teacher-led school prayer that earlier courts had found unconstitutional when clearly state-sponsored.

The guidance was issued the same day as the National Prayer Breakfast and fit a coordinated pattern of February 2026 actions deploying federal institutional authority to advance Christian nationalist policy goals in public education. It built on the administration's earlier executive orders establishing the White House Faith Office and the Religious Liberty Commission, forming part of a systematic effort to reshape the relationship between government and Christianity across multiple federal departments simultaneously.
