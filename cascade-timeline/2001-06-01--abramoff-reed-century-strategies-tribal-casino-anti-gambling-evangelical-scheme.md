Between approximately 1999 and 2004 — with the scheme's operational core running through 2001-2003 — lobbyist Jack Abramoff runs a fraudulent double-dealing operation against Native American tribes using Ralph Reed's evangelical anti-gambling network as the weapon. The scheme works as follows: Abramoff's firm (first Preston Gates & Ellis, then Greenberg Traurig) gets hired to lobby on behalf of tribes seeking casino rights; Abramoff then secretly routes money through Grover Norquist's Americans for Tax Reform to Reed's Century Strategies; Reed activates his evangelical contacts to organize anti-gambling campaigns opposing competing tribal casinos; Abramoff then goes back to the original clients explaining he needs more money to counter the opposition he himself orchestrated.

The financial scale: the Senate Indian Affairs Committee investigation finds that Abramoff and associates charge tribes an estimated $85 million in fees. Ralph Reed is paid more than $4 million through intermediaries — a Mississippi Choctaw official tells the Senate panel that the money is routed through third parties to satisfy Reed's "political concerns," because Reed "did not want to be paid directly by a tribe with gaming interests." The money flows through Americans for Tax Reform and other conservative organizations to reach Century Strategies without visible tribal fingerprints.

**The structural significance for CN lineage:** The Abramoff-Reed scheme reveals what the professionalization of evangelical political infrastructure had produced: a monetizable anti-gambling constituency that could be activated against competitor interests while maintaining the appearance of moral principle. Reed's evangelical contacts — the same church networks that had distributed 33 million voter guides in 1994 — become tools of commercial lobbying fraud when routed through the for-profit consulting operation Reed had built with the skills the Christian Coalition gave him.

The scandal breaks publicly in 2005 and goes to Senate investigation in 2006. Reed, seeking the Georgia Republican lieutenant governorship, loses the primary in 2006 partly due to scandal association. Abramoff pleads guilty to fraud, tax evasion, and conspiracy in January 2006. Grover Norquist's Americans for Tax Reform is investigated for its role as conduit. The scandal demonstrates the corruption risk built into the evangelical-GOP-lobbying complex: the same trusted networks that make evangelical mobilization effective for political campaigns also make them effective for commercial fraud when controlled by operators willing to exploit the trust.

Reed reconstitutes his operation as the Faith and Freedom Coalition in 2009, positioning himself as rehabilitated evangelical political infrastructure for the Tea Party era. The Abramoff scandal does not end Reed's career — it ends one phase of it and forces him to rebuild on different organizational ground.
