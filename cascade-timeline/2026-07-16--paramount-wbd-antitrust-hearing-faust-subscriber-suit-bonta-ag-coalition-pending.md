**Pre-event entry — research date 2026-06-11.** A hearing in **Faust et al. v. Paramount Skydance Corp. and Warner Bros. Discovery** is scheduled for **July 16, 2026** in the **U.S. District Court for the Northern District of California (Oakland)**. The case is a private subscriber antitrust action filed April 30, 2026 by five streaming subscribers — Pamela Faust, Len Marazzo, Lisa McCarthy, Deborah Rubinsohn, and Gary Talewsky, represented by Bay Area attorney Joseph Alioto. The suit alleges that Paramount Skydance's proposed **$110 billion acquisition of Warner Bros. Discovery** violates **Section 7 of the Clayton Antitrust Act** by substantially reducing competition across three defined markets: streaming services, theatrical film distribution, and national television news. Plaintiffs allege the combined entity would control roughly **24% of theatrical distribution** and gain outsized leverage over content creators, producers, and consumers.

**Paramount's motion to dismiss** (filed approximately June 2026) characterizes the action as a "clumsy attempt to politicize antitrust litigation, untethered to any established antitrust principles or law," arguing the merger will "increase investment, expand content offerings and strengthen Paramount's ability to compete" against larger streaming platforms. Paramount is represented by Jeffrey Kessler, co-executive chairman at Winston Taylor. The July 16 hearing is the procedural milestone for that motion.

**Concurrent state-AG track — Bonta-led coalition.** Separate from the private subscriber suit, California AG Rob Bonta leads a state-AG coalition of approximately 10 states investigating the deal independently. As of early June 2026, senior officials across these states were actively **drafting a complaint**, with Democratic-led states including **California, New York, Washington, Oregon, Nevada, Colorado, Connecticut, and Massachusetts**, and Republican AGs from **Tennessee and Pennsylvania** also involved. Bonta stated publicly: "We cannot let a couple of mega-corporations currying favor with the president hold control" of content pipelines, and characterized the Trump DOJ as having "abdicated" antitrust enforcement — framing state action as the last institutional check. The AG coalition's complaint had not been filed as of this entry's research date (2026-06-11); filing was expected "as soon as this month" (June 2026).

**Structural significance.** The July 16 hearing arrives at a critical juncture: Paramount aims to close the WBD acquisition **by July 2026** (hard deadline September 30 with $7 billion termination fee at risk), while the EU decision was expected in early July and international clearances remained pending. The subscriber suit is the first filed legal action; the AG coalition complaint, if filed, would be the institutional resistance pathway. The two tracks are procedurally independent but politically symbiotic — Bonta's public "active investigation" posture keeps regulatory pressure on while complaint logistics are finalized. The merger's editorial-capture dimension (CBS News Bari Weiss installation, 60 Minutes anchor firings — [[2026-06-08--cnn-investigation-bari-weiss-cbs-paramount-wbd-merger-link-bonta-active]]) is documented separately but is explicitly part of Bonta's stated concern about consolidated "control" over content pipelines.
