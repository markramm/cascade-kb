# ICE Defies Federal Judge's Release Order, Re-Detains 77-Year-Old Palestinian Lawful Resident

## What Happened

Akram Mahmoud Omar, a 77-year-old lawful permanent resident who came to the United States in 1975 and had lived in Louisiana for roughly five decades, was ordered released from ICE custody by U.S. District Judge Brian A. Jackson (Middle District of Louisiana). Per the reporting, Omar — born in Palestine before the founding of the state of Israel — suffered a heart attack in ICE custody at the facility his lawyers refer to as "Camp 57," and on or about **May 29, 2026** Judge Jackson found ICE had violated his constitutional rights and ordered his immediate release, with an order stating that "ICE shall IMMEDIATELY RELEASE Omar from ICE custody" and "shall not RE-DETAIN or REMOVE Omar from the United States."

According to The Intercept, The Lens, and the Louisiana Illuminator, ICE then **re-detained Omar without warning and moved to place him on a deportation flight** — directly contravening the standing court order — blocking him from arranging his affairs or saying goodbye to family. An attorney with the National Immigration Project characterized the episode as part of a pattern: "Federal courts are now constantly dealing with flagrant violations of judicial orders by ICE... This level of cruelty and disrespect for federal courts is the rule, not the exception."

(Attribution note: the precise sequence of re-detention and the status of the deportation flight are drawn from the cited press accounts and counsel statements; this entry does not assert a final contempt finding, which was not documented in the sourcing as of June 13, 2026.)

## Why It Matters

This is a **judicial-defiance** event, not merely a harsh-enforcement event. The structurally significant fact is not that an elderly long-resident was targeted, but that ICE **acted against an explicit, written federal-court release order** — the executive enforcement arm overriding a co-equal branch's direct command in an individual case. When an agency re-detains a person a judge has ordered freed, the operative constraint is no longer law but whether the agency chooses to comply. That is the accountability-elimination pattern at the level of the rule of law itself: the court order becomes advisory.

It converges with two other documented June 2026 detention-lane patterns — the disappearance of detainees through opaque inter-facility transfers (see [[2026-06-01--marshall-project-ice-transfers-disappear-immigrants-attorneys-opacity-architecture]]) and the elimination of post-release death reporting — each of which removes a different external check on the detention system. Re-detention-in-defiance removes the judicial check; transfer-opacity removes the locational/attorney check; death-reporting erasure removes the mortality check. Convergence, not coordination: independent administrative moves that each subtract a layer of oversight.
