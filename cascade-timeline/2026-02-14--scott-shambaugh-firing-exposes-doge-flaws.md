Scott Shambaugh, a mechanical engineer at the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) and sole maintainer of the widely-used open-source Python library "solidpython," was fired in early February 2025 as part of DOGE's sweeping federal workforce reductions, exposing the indiscriminate nature of the purge. Shambaugh's case became emblematic of DOGE's flawed approach because his probationary status was a bureaucratic technicality, not a reflection of his value or competence—he was a skilled engineer whose open-source contributions demonstrated initiative and technical capability that most organizations would fight to retain.

The firing revealed that DOGE's approach relied on crude proxies—probationary status, recent hiring date, position classification—rather than any meaningful assessment of what individual employees actually do, what they contribute, or what would be lost if they were removed. Critics, including former government officials, union leaders, and growing numbers of Republican lawmakers, argued that DOGE operated "more like a chainsaw" than the "scalpel that will cut government waste" it claimed to be. The automated, across-the-board nature of DOGE-driven firings made no distinction between redundant administrative hires and critical technical contributors.

Shambaugh's work at NIST—an agency whose mission is to promote U.S. innovation and industrial competitiveness through measurement science and standards—was directly aligned with the kind of technical excellence the United States needs to maintain its edge in advanced manufacturing and technology. The firing targeted probationary federal employees as the lowest-risk targets for reduction because they lacked civil service protections afforded to tenured workers, with the assumption these employees were most expendable and least embedded in critical operations. The case raised serious questions about whether the administration's "efficiency" approach was undermining critical technical expertise within the federal government, with experts warning that "as challenging as 2025 was, we can expect even harder days ahead" as the impacts of thoughtless workforce cuts are felt more deeply.
