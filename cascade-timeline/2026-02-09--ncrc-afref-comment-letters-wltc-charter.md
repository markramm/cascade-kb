On February 9, 2026, the National Community Reinvestment Coalition (NCRC) and Americans for Financial Reform Education Fund (AFREF) both filed formal public-comment letters with the OCC opposing the WLTC Holdings, LLC application (OCC docket 2026-Charter-344521; regulations.gov docket OCC-2026-0100-0004) to establish World Liberty Trust Company, National Association.

NCRC's letter raised eight grounds for opposition:

1. Conflicts of interest — the applicant is co-founded by Trump family members and administration-proximate principals; the OCC is headed by a presidentially-removable official
2. Lack of OCC authority — the proposed bank's stablecoin-issuance and payments activities exceed the National Bank Act's trust-charter scope, which should be limited to fiduciary activities
3. Regulatory arbitrage — the structure grants banking credibility while avoiding FDIC deposit insurance and consolidated Federal Reserve holding-company supervision
4. Lost Community Reinvestment — trust banks are not subject to Community Reinvestment Act obligations, potentially diverting billions from community development
5. Consumer harm — a federal charter creates a false appearance of safety for uninsured stablecoin holders
6. Weakened oversight — federal preemption displaces state-level consumer protections
7. Safety and soundness risks — federal approval of weakly-regulated stablecoin activity increases systemic risk
8. Premature issuance — NCRC urged a moratorium until GENIUS Act implementing regulations are finalized

NCRC also requested that the OCC extend the standard 30-day comment period for national trust applications to 90 days. AFREF filed a parallel comment letter the same day.

The comment letters constitute the formal civil-society record against WLTC's application. Together with Senator Warren's letters ([[2026-01-13--warren-presses-occ-halt-wlfi-bank-charter]], [[2026-01-23--occ-rebuffs-warren-wlfi-charter-delay]]), they position both legislative-branch and civil-society opposition on the OCC's administrative record — relevant for any subsequent Administrative Procedure Act litigation.
