Major work stoppages in the United States decline to approximately 16 per year by the 2010s, representing a 95% collapse from the average of 300 major strikes annually before Reagan's 1981 PATCO strike-breaking. The dramatic reduction in strike activity reflects workers' rational assessment that striking under current legal framework means permanent job loss rather than collective bargaining leverage, as permanent striker replacement (normalized after PATCO) combined with toothless NLRB enforcement makes strikes effectively suicidal for organized labor.

The strike collapse follows directly from corporate exploitation of Taft-Hartley's permanent replacement provision: Phelps Dodge (1983), Hormel (1985), International Paper, and Chicago Tribune demonstrate that companies can maintain operations indefinitely with replacement workers, then decertify unions through replacement worker votes. With NLRB unable to prevent permanent replacement and taking 2-3 years to resolve unfair labor practice charges, strikes lose their economic leverage while exposing workers to permanent termination.

The 95% decline in major strikes represents the effective elimination of labor's most powerful weapon, completing the transformation of collective bargaining from a genuine negotiation backed by strike threat into a formality where workers accept management terms or face replacement. Where strikes historically provided workers leverage to demand wage increases matching productivity growth (1948-1979), the collapse of strike activity after PATCO enables the productivity-pay divergence: productivity grows 80% from 1979-2024 while wages increase only 29%, with all productivity gains captured by capital. The strike decline from 300 to 16 annually measures the success of coordinated corporate labor destruction: PATCO precedent (1981), permanent replacement normalization (1980s), NLRB regulatory capture, right-to-work expansion (2010-2017), and weak penalties make striking economically irrational, destroying the foundation of collective bargaining power.
