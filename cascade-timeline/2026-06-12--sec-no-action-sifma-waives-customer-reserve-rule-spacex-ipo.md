# SEC No-Action Letter Waives the Customer-Reserve Safeguard for the SpaceX IPO

## What Happened

As **SpaceX** prepared what was being billed as a historic IPO (with reporting referencing a valuation in the ~$1.77 trillion range), the **SEC granted a no-action letter to SIFMA** — the Securities Industry and Financial Markets Association, the broker-dealer industry's lobbying body — stating that the agency **would not recommend enforcement action** against brokerages that **temporarily reduce the cash held in their customer-reserve accounts** for transactions tied to the SpaceX offering.

The customer-reserve requirement (the rule that forces broker-dealers to segregate and hold customer cash in a special reserve so that customers are protected if the firm fails) exists precisely to safeguard everyday investors' assets in a bankruptcy. The no-action relief — sought by SIFMA and granted by the SEC — relaxed that buffer during the expected trading frenzy, allowing brokerages to earn fees on far more transactions. Experts cited by The Lever warned the waiver could jeopardize the assets of ordinary investors caught in the hype.

In parallel, **Sen. Elizabeth Warren** (1) urged the SEC to **delay** the IPO over valuation, governance, and investor-protection concerns, and (2) **pressed major index providers** over rule changes that could rush SpaceX shares into index funds — and thus into Americans' retirement accounts — while waiving the usual inclusion safeguards.

(Attribution note: a no-action letter is **not** a formal rule change or rescission of the customer-reserve rule; it is a statement of non-enforcement intent for a defined circumstance. That is the precise mechanism — the protection stays on the books while the agency declines to enforce it for this event.)

## Why It Matters

This is **regulatory capture by selective non-enforcement** — the cleanest form, because nothing is repealed and no vote is taken. The investor-protection rule remains in the code; the regulator simply agrees, at the industry's request, not to enforce it for the single most lucrative offering of the cycle. The beneficiary is a politically connected issuer (SpaceX / Elon Musk) and the broker-dealers handling the flood; the party whose protection is suspended is the retail customer.

It fits the corpus pattern in which **rules survive on paper while the enforcement that gives them force is waived case-by-case for favored actors** — the same shape as the GENIUS Act AML framework's verified-absent enforcement gaps (see the June 9 GENIUS Act entry) and the export-control / CFIUS-waiver pattern around frontier-AI equity stakes ([[2026-06-01--mgx-tahnoon-anthropic-stake-triple-frontier-ai-lab-equity-no-cfius]]). The throughline: a captured regulatory apparatus increasingly governs by deciding *whom not to regulate*.
