On April 2, 2025 — a date Trump dubbed "Liberation Day" — the president signed Executive Order 14257 declaring a national emergency over the trade deficit and imposing sweeping reciprocal tariffs under the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA). The order set a universal 10% tariff floor on nearly all imports beginning April 5, with differential rates up to 50% for major trading partners effective April 9. The tariffs constituted the largest U.S. tax increase as a percentage of GDP since 1993.

## Consumer Price Impact

Research from multiple independent sources documented the direct cost to American households:

- **Tax Foundation:** Average household cost of $1,000 in 2025, rising to $1,300 in 2026
- **Yale Budget Lab:** Projected average annual cost of $2,400 per household if policies remained in place
- **New York Federal Reserve:** Americans bore 94% of tariff costs as of August 2025
- **Imported goods prices:** Rose approximately 4.0% between March and September 2025
- **Domestic goods prices:** Rose 2.0% in the same period as producers raised prices under reduced import competition

Harvard Business School Pricing Lab data showed especially steep increases for clothing accessories, jewelry, household tools, toys, personal care products, and small appliances. Food prices rose 1.6% in the month following the announcement alone — equivalent to an entire year of prior grocery inflation.

## Cost Pass-Through Acceleration

In 2025, businesses absorbed roughly 80% of tariff costs to maintain competitiveness. By early 2026, analysts projected that ratio would reverse, with consumers absorbing 80% of costs as businesses exhausted their capacity to absorb margin compression. This delayed pass-through meant the full consumer impact of Liberation Day tariffs was still escalating more than a year later.

## Supreme Court Intervention

In February 2026, the Supreme Court ruled that most of Trump's emergency tariffs were unconstitutional, finding the IEEPA did not authorize tariffs of this scope. The ruling triggered an unprecedented process of issuing refunds to affected importers, though the consumer price increases that had already occurred were not reversible.

## Capture Significance

The Liberation Day tariffs represent a direct mechanism of economic extraction from American households — a regressive tax imposed by executive fiat under emergency powers never intended for trade policy. The tariffs disproportionately affected lower-income households who spend a higher percentage of income on consumer goods. The use of IEEPA emergency authority to circumvent Congress's constitutional power over tariffs and trade paralleled the administration's broader pattern of claiming emergency powers to bypass legislative checks.
