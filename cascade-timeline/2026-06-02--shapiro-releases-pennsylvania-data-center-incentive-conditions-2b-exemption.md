Governor Josh Shapiro released full standards for Pennsylvania's data center tax-incentive program on June 2, 2026, as the June 30 state budget deadline approached. The existing sales-tax exemption for data center equipment — currently unconditional for projects meeting financial benchmarks — is projected to cost the state over $2 billion by mid-2031. With some legislators pushing to repeal the exemption outright, Shapiro's counter is to attach conditions: developers must bring their own power to the grid or pay for the energy they use, commit to transparency, hire and train local workers, and follow environmental protections. The fight is whether those conditions have enforcement teeth or function as a fig leaf preserving a $2 billion giveaway to hyperscalers.

This is the incentive-conditioning middle path between the clawback moratoriums in Arizona and Illinois ([[2026-06-22--arizona-illinois-data-center-tax-moratoriums-300-bills-nationally]]) and New York's outright statewide pause ([[2026-06-03--new-york-legislature-passes-statewide-data-center-moratorium-s10642]]) — a Democratic governor trying to preserve recruitment while answering the fiscal-capture critique ([[2026-06-11--data-center-fiscal-capture-tax-exemptions-state-budgets-electoral-salience]]). The load-bearing question is the "bring your own power" requirement: whether it has real enforcement or whether developers escape it via power-purchase-agreement structures that still shift grid costs onto ratepayers, the same cost-shift the record PJM capacity auction exposed ([[2025-11-01--pjm-capacity-auction-record-data-center-40-percent-costs]]).
