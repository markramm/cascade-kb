A Stateline investigation published May 13, 2026 documented a new red-state mechanism: laws requiring state and local agencies to verify the immigration status of people applying for federal, state, or local benefits, effectively deputizing social-service and benefits-intake workers as immigration screeners. Tennessee's law requires all state and local agencies to verify applicants' immigration status and report those found to be in the country illegally to the legislature and the state's new immigration-enforcement agency; it authorizes the state attorney general to investigate non-compliance and threatens jail time or loss of state funding for workers or agencies that fail to comply. Indiana and Wyoming also require agencies to report unverifiable applicants to federal authorities — Indiana specifically when it cannot verify the status of any household member on a SNAP application — and Utah enacted a parallel benefit-verification law in 2026. Tennessee's broader immigration package was, per the reporting, crafted in coordination with the White House, specifically Deputy Chief of Staff Stephen Miller.

This is a second-front state-capture mechanism distinct from the law-enforcement deputization of Tennessee's HB2219 287(g) mandate (`2026-05-10--tennessee-governor-lee-signs-hb2219-95-sheriffs-287g-first-state-mandate`): it deputizes the welfare-administration workforce, with AG enforcement and funding-loss penalties for non-compliant workers. The structural pattern is the conversion of every routine state-touch surface — police, benefits intake, and voter rolls (cf. the DHS SAVE purge, `2026-05-04--dhs-save-voter-roll-purge-blitz-ohio-texas-idaho-2026-midterms`) — into an immigration-status checkpoint, with the federal layer (Miller) coordinating the state legislative design.
