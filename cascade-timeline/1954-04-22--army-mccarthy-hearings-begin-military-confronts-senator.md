On April 22, 1954, the Army-McCarthy hearings began—36 days of televised proceedings that exposed Senator Joseph McCarthy's methods to a national audience and began his political downfall. The hearings were triggered by the Army's March 11 report charging McCarthy and his chief counsel Roy Cohn with improperly pressuring military officials to give preferential treatment to G. David Schine, a wealthy former McCarthy aide who had been drafted.

The conflict originated with Schine's November 1953 induction into the Army as a private. Cohn, who had a close personal relationship with Schine, allegedly made hundreds of phone calls and visits pressuring Army officials for special assignments and privileges for his friend. When the Army refused, McCarthy's subcommittee began investigating alleged Communist infiltration of the Army—widely seen as retaliation.

The hearings, broadcast live by ABC and DuMont networks, reached an estimated 20 million viewers. Americans watched McCarthy bully witnesses, interrupt proceedings, and make reckless accusations. His famous cry of "Point of order, Mr. Chairman!" became a national catchphrase for obstruction. The contrast between McCarthy's aggressive tactics and Army counsel Joseph Welch's measured responses damaged McCarthy's public image.

The hearings' climactic moment came on June 9, when McCarthy attacked a young lawyer in Welch's firm who had once belonged to a lawyers' guild with alleged Communist ties. Welch's response—"Have you no sense of decency, sir, at long last?"—crystallized public revulsion at McCarthy's methods. The gallery applauded, and McCarthy sat stunned.

The Army-McCarthy hearings demonstrated that McCarthy's power depended on his ability to control proceedings and avoid scrutiny. Televised hearings stripped away that control, exposing his methods to public evaluation. Though the hearings ended inconclusively in technical terms, they destroyed McCarthy's political standing and paved the way for his December 1954 Senate censure. The episode showed how institutions—in this case the Army and television networks—could resist and ultimately defeat authoritarian political tactics.
