On August 17, 1992, Patrick J. Buchanan addressed the Republican National Convention in Houston and delivered what becomes known as the "Culture War Speech" — the foundational rhetorical document of the Christian right's 1990s middle period. The money line: "There is a religious war going on in our country for the soul of America. It is a cultural war, as critical to the kind of nation we shall be as the Cold War itself."

Buchanan had challenged incumbent President George H.W. Bush in the Republican primary, winning 37% of the New Hampshire vote on a platform of social conservatism and "America First" nationalism. His convention speech — given the prime-time slot in exchange for his endorsement of Bush — made explicit what the Christian Coalition's voter guides had been encoding implicitly: the contest between conservatives and liberals was not merely political but theological, a zero-sum war for the nation's spiritual character.

The speech named the stakes: abortion, homosexual rights, women in combat, school prayer, "radical feminism." Buchanan described the Clintons as proponents of "abortion on demand, a litmus test for the Supreme Court, homosexual rights, discrimination against religious schools, women in combat units." He framed Democratic positions not as policy disagreements but as assaults on America's religious identity.

**The structural significance is in the framing, not the policy.** Buchanan did not invent these issues — Falwell's Moral Majority had worked them since 1979. What he did was give the coalition a single unified register: *war*. Not civic debate, not legislative negotiation, but a "struggle for the soul of America" in which the Christian right was the defending army. This war framing — which Pat Robertson, James Dobson, and Ralph Reed's Christian Coalition would absorb and amplify through the decade — is the rhetorical architecture the evangelical movement inhabits from 1992 forward. Buchanan lost the primary and Bush lost the general election, but the culture-war vocabulary that Buchanan crystallized became the permanent operating system of the Christian nationalist movement: every election cycle a battle, every policy concession a defeat, every compromise a betrayal.

The speech is also the structural bridge between the Moral Majority's 1979-1989 first phase and the Christian Coalition's 1989-1997 operational peak. Falwell had built the organizational infrastructure; Buchanan gave it a unifying enemy narrative that carried through the Promise Keepers mobilization (1990-1997), the DOMA campaign (1996), the Dobson-ultimatum to the GOP (1998), and the Bush "values voter" reelection strategy (2004).
